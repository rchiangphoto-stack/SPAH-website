Cat Dental Disease in Alhambra: Signs, Cleaning, and What Owners Miss

Dental disease is the most common health problem in cats — more common than kidney disease, more common than hyperthyroidism, and far more common than most owners realize. By age three, the majority of cats have some degree of tartar and gum inflammation. By age five or six, many have dental disease significant enough to cause pain. And through most of it, the cat gives almost no sign that anything is wrong.

At South Pasadena Animal Hospital in Alhambra, dental assessments are part of every wellness exam. We perform professional dental cleanings under anesthesia and handle extractions when teeth are too diseased to save. Here's what cat owners in the SGV need to understand about feline dental health.

Why cats hide dental pain

Cats don't cry out or whimper when their teeth hurt — not usually, and not obviously. The instinct is to continue eating as normally as possible, because an animal that stops eating in the wild doesn't survive. So cats adapt. They chew more on one side. They swallow food without chewing it. They start preferring wet food over dry. They groom less, especially the lower back and tail. They become slightly less interactive. None of these changes alarm most owners into thinking "dental problem" — and yet each one is a signal.

This is why veterinary dental assessments matter more in cats than in almost any other patient. We're looking for what the cat isn't showing you.

The stages of feline dental disease

Dental disease in cats progresses in stages:

  • Stage 1 (Gingivitis) — Plaque and tartar accumulate, causing inflammation at the gumline. The gums look red and may bleed easily when touched. This stage is reversible with a thorough cleaning.
  • Stage 2 (Early periodontal disease) — The gums begin to pull away from the teeth (recession), forming pockets where bacteria accumulate. Bone loss begins. Partially reversible with treatment.
  • Stage 3–4 (Advanced periodontal disease) — Significant bone loss, tooth loosening, abscess formation. Severely affected teeth typically require extraction. The bacteria involved can enter the bloodstream and affect the heart, kidneys, and liver over time.

Tooth resorption: the cat-specific problem

Tooth resorption is a condition almost unique to cats. It's a process in which the body gradually breaks down the tooth's structure — starting at the root and working outward. Over time, the crown of the tooth erodes away and the root is absorbed into the bone. It's extremely painful at the active stages, and it affects a significant portion of adult cats.

Owners can sometimes see a pink or reddish lesion at the gumline where the tooth is breaking down, but in many cases there's nothing visible on the surface. Dental X-rays are the only reliable way to diagnose tooth resorption — which is one of the reasons dental X-rays are a standard part of a thorough dental cleaning, not an optional add-on. The treatment is extraction of the affected tooth.

A cat with active tooth resorption may eat normally enough to fool their owner. Once the tooth is removed, many owners describe a noticeable shift — the cat becomes more playful, more affectionate, more comfortable — and realize in retrospect that the cat had been in significant pain for some time.

Signs of dental disease in cats

The symptoms range from obvious to nearly invisible:

  • Bad breath (persistent, not just post-meal)
  • Visible yellow or brown tartar on the teeth
  • Red, puffy, or bleeding gums
  • Drooling, sometimes with blood
  • Pawing at the mouth or face
  • Chewing on one side only
  • Preference for soft food or reluctance to eat dry food
  • Dropping food while eating
  • Weight loss
  • Reduced grooming
  • Irritability or decreased tolerance for face handling

Many cats show none of these signs until dental disease is already advanced. Annual or twice-yearly dental assessments from a vet are the most reliable way to catch problems early.

What a professional dental cleaning at SPAH involves

A professional dental cleaning requires general anesthesia. This is not optional — it's not about convenience. Anesthesia is what makes it possible to scale below the gumline safely, probe each tooth individually, take full-mouth dental X-rays, and treat problems found during the procedure. An awake cat cannot have any of this done properly, and "anesthesia-free dentals" performed at grooming facilities only clean visible surface tartar — they don't treat the disease below the gumline where it matters.

Here's what a cleaning at SPAH includes:

  • Pre-anesthetic bloodwork — ensures your cat's kidneys, liver, and blood values are within safe ranges before going under. Standard for all dental patients.
  • IV catheter and fluids — supports blood pressure and allows for immediate medication delivery if needed during the procedure.
  • Full-mouth dental X-rays — reveals what's happening below the gumline: root resorption, bone loss, abscesses, retained roots.
  • Ultrasonic scaling and polishing — removes tartar above and below the gumline, followed by polishing to smooth the enamel surface.
  • Periodontal probing — each tooth is individually probed to check pocket depth and assess attachment.
  • Extractions (if needed) — teeth that are too diseased, resorbing, or infected are extracted. Sutures close the extraction sites.
  • Pain management — local nerve blocks and take-home pain medication as needed.

After a cleaning, most cats recover quickly and are eating normally the same evening or the next day. For cats with extractions, soft food is recommended for a few days while the sites heal.

How often do cats need dental cleanings?

It depends on the individual cat. Some cats accumulate tartar rapidly and need annual cleanings; others can go two years between procedures. We assess dental health at every wellness exam and recommend timing based on what we find — not a fixed schedule that applies uniformly to every cat. Our pricing page lists dental cleaning fees.

Home dental care for cats

Daily tooth brushing with a pet-safe toothpaste is the most effective at-home prevention. Getting a cat to accept tooth brushing takes patience — starting young, using positive reinforcement, and working up slowly from letting the cat taste the toothpaste to eventually accepting a brush. It's not realistic for every cat, but even several times a week makes a difference.

Dental chews, water additives, and specific dental diets can help reduce plaque accumulation but don't replace professional cleanings. Ask us at your cat's next visit what home care approach makes the most sense for their specific situation.

Questions about cat dental care in Alhambra

How do I know if my cat has dental disease?

Common signs include bad breath, red gums, tartar buildup, drooling, pawing at the mouth, preference for soft food, and chewing on one side. Many cats show minimal signs — a vet dental assessment at each wellness exam is the most reliable way to catch problems early.

Does my cat need anesthesia for a dental cleaning?

Yes. Proper dental care requires anesthesia to scale below the gumline, take X-rays, probe each tooth, and safely treat any problems found. Anesthesia-free dentals only clean visible surface tartar and don't address disease where it actually develops.

What is tooth resorption in cats?

Tooth resorption is a painful condition where the tooth structure breaks down from the inside, starting at the root. It's very common in adult cats and is diagnosed with dental X-rays. The treatment is extraction of the affected tooth.

How often does my cat need a dental cleaning?

Every 1–2 years for most cats, depending on how quickly tartar builds up. We assess dental health at every wellness exam and recommend timing based on what we find.
