Urinary Problems in Cats & Dogs: Signs, Causes & What Vets Do

Urinary problems are one of the most emotionally stressful things pet owners encounter — few things are more alarming than watching your cat crouch in the litter box in obvious distress, or finding blood in your dog's urine. These symptoms can range from a simple infection that responds quickly to antibiotics, to a life-threatening urinary obstruction that requires emergency surgery. Understanding the difference — and acting appropriately quickly — can be the difference between a straightforward recovery and a crisis.

Signs of a Urinary Problem in Your Pet

Recognizing urinary symptoms early makes treatment easier and outcomes better. Watch for:

  • Frequent trips to the litter box or outside with small amounts of urine produced each time
  • Straining, squatting, or crying during urination
  • Blood in the urine — pink, red, or dark brown coloring
  • Urinating in unusual locations outside the litter box or normal routine (house soiling)
  • Licking at the genitals excessively
  • Strong or unusual urine odor
  • Lethargy, loss of appetite, or vomiting alongside urinary symptoms
  • A distended or painful abdomen — especially in cats

The most critical observation: is your pet actually producing urine? A cat or dog straining with small amounts of urine is different from a pet making no urine at all. No urine production alongside straining is a potential blockage — an emergency.

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD)

FLUTD is a broad term for conditions affecting the lower urinary tract in cats — the bladder and urethra. It's one of the most common presentations in feline medicine, accounting for a significant portion of veterinary visits in cats under 10 years old.

Feline Idiopathic Cystitis

The most common FLUTD diagnosis in young to middle-aged cats is feline idiopathic cystitis (FIC) — bladder inflammation with no identifiable bacterial cause. FIC is closely linked to stress. Environmental changes, household tension, boredom, and multi-cat household dynamics can all trigger episodes. Symptoms include straining, frequent trips to the box, and bloody urine — identical in appearance to a bacterial UTI, but bacteria are absent on urinalysis.

Management focuses on reducing stress (environmental enrichment, litter box optimization, Feliway pheromone diffusers), increasing water intake (feeding wet food, water fountains), and sometimes veterinary-prescribed medications. Most episodes resolve within a week, but recurrence is common without addressing the underlying stress triggers.

Urinary Crystals and Bladder Stones

Crystals form in the urine when certain minerals precipitate out of solution. Struvite crystals are associated with alkaline urine and sometimes bacterial infection; calcium oxalate crystals are associated with acidic urine and breed predisposition (Persian, Himalayan, Ragdoll cats are at higher risk). Crystals can aggregate into stones (uroliths) that irritate the bladder wall and occasionally obstruct the urethra.

Diagnosis requires urinalysis (to identify crystal type) and X-rays or ultrasound to detect stones. Treatment depends on the crystal type — struvite may dissolve with dietary management; oxalate stones typically require surgical removal.

Bacterial UTI in Cats

True bacterial urinary tract infections are actually less common in cats than in dogs — particularly in young cats with FLUTD. They become more common in older cats and in cats with underlying conditions like diabetes or kidney disease that alter urine characteristics. Diagnosis requires a urine culture, not just a urinalysis, to identify the specific organism and select the appropriate antibiotic.

A Blocked Cat Is a Life-Threatening Emergency

Male cats are anatomically predisposed to urethral obstruction — their urethra is long and narrow, making it more easily blocked by crystals, mucus plugs, or stones. A blocked cat cannot urinate at all. Toxins accumulate in the bloodstream, potassium levels rise dangerously, and the bladder can rupture if the obstruction is not relieved.

Signs of a blocked cat: going in and out of the litter box repeatedly with no urine production, crying or vocalizing in pain, lethargic and not eating, a firm distended abdomen, and in advanced cases, collapse and unresponsiveness.

A urethral obstruction is fatal without treatment, typically within 24–48 hours. If your male cat is straining with no urine and showing any of these signs, do not wait — go to a veterinarian or emergency clinic immediately. Call ahead: (626) 441-1314.

Treatment involves hospitalization, urethral catheterization to relieve the obstruction, IV fluids, and monitoring. Most cats recover fully with prompt treatment. After a first blockage, dietary modification and increased water intake are strongly recommended to reduce recurrence risk.

Urinary Problems in Dogs

Dogs are less prone to urethral obstruction than cats, but urinary problems are still common. Female dogs are anatomically predisposed to ascending urinary tract infections due to their shorter, wider urethra. Common canine urinary conditions include:

  • Bacterial UTI — the most common urinary diagnosis in dogs. Signs include frequent urination, straining, blood in urine, and house soiling. Diagnosis requires urinalysis and urine culture. Complete the prescribed antibiotic course — partial treatment leads to resistance and recurrence.
  • Bladder stones (urolithiasis) — can cause chronic recurrent UTIs, hematuria (blood in urine), and straining. Some stone types can be dissolved with dietary management; others require surgical removal. X-rays and ultrasound are used for detection.
  • Incontinence — urinary leakage, especially during sleep or rest, is common in spayed female dogs and some older dogs. It is generally not a UTI; it reflects reduced urethral sphincter tone. Highly treatable with medication.
  • Prostate disease — intact male dogs can develop benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH), prostatic cysts, or prostatitis, all of which can affect urinary function. Neutering resolves BPH.

Any dog with hematuria (blood in urine) should have a urinalysis to check for infection, crystals, and abnormal cells. In older dogs — particularly intact females — bladder tumors can also present with hematuria and are identified on ultrasound.

Urinary Issues in Exotic Pets

Rabbits normally produce cloudy white or reddish urine due to calcium carbonate and plant pigments — this does not mean blood is present. True blood in rabbit urine (hematuria) can result from uterine disease in intact females, bladder sludge (calcium carbonate accumulation), or bladder stones. Rabbits with "sludgy" urine may have a painful, difficult time urinating and may produce very little; this requires veterinary evaluation and often dietary modification. Intact female rabbits have a high rate of uterine adenocarcinoma after age three — one of the reasons spaying is strongly recommended.

Guinea pigs are prone to urinary tract infections and bladder stones (most commonly calcium oxalate and calcium carbonate). Signs include straining, crying when urinating, and blood in urine. High-calcium diets — including excessive leafy greens — contribute to stone formation. Diagnosis and management require veterinary care; stones sometimes require surgery.

Male guinea pigs can develop impaction of the perineal sac (a skin pouch near the anus) that may be confused with urinary problems. Regular cleaning of the perineal sac in senior males helps prevent this.

What the Vet Visit Involves

A urinary workup at our Alhambra clinic typically begins with a urinalysis — checking urine specific gravity, pH, protein, glucose, blood, white cells, and sediment under a microscope. If infection is suspected, a urine culture and sensitivity test identifies the organism and guides antibiotic selection.

Abdominal X-rays detect most bladder stones (though some are radiolucent and only visible on ultrasound). Ultrasound provides more detailed imaging of the bladder wall, kidneys, and surrounding structures. Blood work is often added for older patients or those with systemic signs.

Treatment ranges from a short antibiotic course for a simple UTI, to hospitalization and catheterization for a blocked cat, to surgery for bladder stones. Follow-up urinalysis after a UTI treatment confirms resolution — a step that is frequently skipped and leads to under-treated or recurring infections.

Frequently Asked Questions

My cat is straining in the litter box — is this an emergency?

If your cat is producing small amounts of urine: same-day vet visit. If your cat is straining and producing NO urine, or is crying in pain and lethargic: go immediately. A blocked cat is life-threatening. Do not wait overnight.

What is FLUTD and how is it treated?

FLUTD is an umbrella term for conditions affecting the lower urinary tract in cats. The most common form is idiopathic cystitis — stress-triggered inflammation without bacterial infection. Treatment focuses on environmental enrichment, stress reduction, dietary modification, and increased water intake. Recurrence is common without addressing underlying triggers.

Can stress cause urinary problems in cats?

Yes. Feline idiopathic cystitis is directly linked to stress in many cats. Changes in household routine, new pets, and environmental factors can all trigger a flare. Management focuses on reducing stress and supporting urinary health through wet food, water fountains, and environmental enrichment.

How do I know if my dog has a UTI?

Signs include frequent urination with small amounts produced, straining or crying during urination, blood-tinged urine, and licking at the genitals. Female dogs are more prone to UTIs. Diagnosis requires urinalysis and urine culture. Book an exam for accurate diagnosis and the right antibiotic.

What causes crystals in cat or dog urine?

Crystals form when certain minerals precipitate out of solution. Common types include struvite and calcium oxalate. Diet, water intake, and genetics all play roles. A urinalysis identifies crystal type; management depends on which type is present and may include dietary change, increased water, or surgery for stones.
