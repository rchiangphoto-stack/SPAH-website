Urinary Problems in Cats & Dogs: Signs, Causes & What Vets Do

Urinary problems bring owners in urgently — and rightly so. A cat going in and out of the litter box, crying, producing nothing — that's one of the more stressful things to watch, and in male cats it can be life-threatening within 24 hours. Blood in a dog's urine is alarming for different reasons. And leakage in a sleeping spayed female is something else entirely.

Same category — urinary symptoms — very different underlying conditions, and very different urgency levels. Here's how we sort through it.

Signs That Point to a Urinary Problem

  • Frequent trips to the litter box or outside, producing small amounts each time
  • Straining, squatting, or crying during urination
  • Pink, red, or dark brown urine
  • Urinating in unusual locations — on the floor, outside the box, in the house
  • Excessive licking at the genitals
  • Strong or different-smelling urine
  • Lethargy, appetite loss, or vomiting alongside urinary signs
  • A distended or painful abdomen

The most important question to answer: is your pet actually producing urine? Straining with some urine coming out is one situation. Straining with no urine coming out is another — that's a potential obstruction, and it's an emergency.

Feline Lower Urinary Tract Disease (FLUTD)

FLUTD covers all conditions affecting the feline bladder and urethra. It's one of the most common presentations in cat medicine, and it comes in several forms that look identical from the outside but have different causes.

Feline Idiopathic Cystitis (FIC)

The most common FLUTD diagnosis in younger and middle-aged cats — bladder inflammation with no bacterial cause. It's directly linked to stress. A new pet. A move. A change in schedule. Multi-cat tension. A bored indoor cat with nothing to do. The signs are the same as a UTI: straining, frequent box visits, bloody urine. But the urinalysis comes back negative for bacteria.

Management: reduce stress through environmental enrichment, litter box optimization (one more box than the number of cats, kept clean), Feliway diffusers, wet food, water fountains. Most episodes resolve within a week. Without addressing the underlying stress triggers, recurrence is very common.

Urinary Crystals and Bladder Stones

Minerals precipitate out of urine under certain conditions and form crystals — struvite in alkaline urine often linked to infection, calcium oxalate in acidic urine with breed predisposition (Persians, Himalayans, Ragdolls). Crystals aggregate into stones that irritate the bladder wall and can obstruct the urethra.

Diagnosis requires urinalysis to identify crystal type, plus X-rays or ultrasound to detect formed stones. Struvite may dissolve with dietary management and addressing the infection. Oxalate stones don't dissolve — they have to be surgically removed.

Bacterial UTI in Cats

Less common in cats than dogs — especially in younger cats presenting with FLUTD signs, where idiopathic cystitis is more likely. True bacterial UTIs become more common in older cats and those with underlying diabetes or kidney disease that changes urine characteristics. Diagnosis requires a urine culture, not just urinalysis — we need to know the specific organism to choose the right antibiotic.

A Blocked Male Cat: Go Now

Male cats have a long, narrow urethra that's much more prone to obstruction by crystals, mucus plugs, or stones than females. A blocked cat cannot urinate at all. Toxins build up in the bloodstream, potassium rises dangerously, and the bladder can rupture if the obstruction isn't relieved.

What it looks like: in and out of the litter box constantly with no urine coming out. Crying or vocalizing in pain. Lethargic, not eating. Firm, distended abdomen. In advanced cases: collapse.

Fatal within 24–48 hours without treatment. If your male cat is straining with no urine production — don't wait to see how he does overnight. Go to a vet or emergency clinic immediately. Call ahead: (626) 441-1314.

Treatment: hospitalization, urethral catheterization, IV fluids, monitoring until stable. Most cats recover fully with prompt care. After a first blockage, dietary modification and increased water intake significantly reduce recurrence risk.

Urinary Problems in Dogs

Dogs obstruct less often than male cats, but urinary issues are still common. Female dogs get UTIs far more often than males — their shorter, wider urethra makes bacterial ascent much easier. Common conditions:

  • Bacterial UTI — the most frequent urinary diagnosis in dogs. Frequent urination, straining, blood in urine, house soiling. Needs urinalysis and urine culture. Finish the antibiotic course — partial treatment leads to resistant recurrence
  • Bladder stones — chronic recurrent infections, blood in urine, straining. Some types dissolve with diet; others need surgery. X-rays and ultrasound for detection
  • Incontinence — leakage during sleep or rest, especially in spayed females and older dogs. Not a UTI. Reflects reduced urethral sphincter tone. Responds well to medication
  • Prostate disease — intact male dogs can develop prostatic hyperplasia, cysts, or prostatitis that affects urinary function. Neutering resolves hyperplasia

Any dog with blood in the urine needs a urinalysis. In older intact females, bladder tumors can present with the same signs as a UTI and are identified on ultrasound.

Urinary Issues in Exotic Pets

Rabbits normally produce cloudy white or reddish urine from calcium carbonate and plant pigments — that's normal for rabbits and doesn't indicate blood. True hematuria in rabbits usually means uterine disease in intact females, bladder sludge (calcium accumulation), or stones. Sludgy urine causes straining and reduced output. Intact females over three have a very high rate of uterine adenocarcinoma — one of the strongest arguments for spaying before age two.

Guinea pigs get UTIs and bladder stones regularly. Signs: straining, crying when urinating, blood in urine. High-calcium diets — excessive leafy greens — contribute to stone formation. Stones in guinea pigs often need surgical removal. Senior male guinea pigs can also develop perineal sac impaction that looks like urinary straining — different problem, different solution.

What the Workup Looks Like

At our Alhambra clinic, we start with a urinalysis — specific gravity, pH, protein, glucose, blood, white cells, and sediment under the microscope. If infection is suspected, a urine culture and sensitivity test identifies the organism and guides antibiotic choice.

Abdominal X-rays detect most bladder stones (some are radiolucent and require ultrasound instead). Ultrasound provides more detailed imaging of the bladder wall, kidneys, and surrounding structures. Bloodwork is added for older patients or those with systemic signs.

Treatment ranges from a short antibiotic course to hospitalization and catheterization to surgery, depending on what we find. One thing worth noting: follow-up urinalysis 5–7 days after completing antibiotics matters. We confirm the infection actually cleared. Most owners skip this. We'd rather they didn't — partial treatment is one of the main reasons UTIs recur.

Frequently Asked Questions

My cat is straining in the litter box — is this an emergency?

Small amounts of urine coming out: same-day vet visit. No urine at all, crying in pain, lethargic: go immediately. A blocked cat can die within 24 hours without treatment. Don't wait overnight on this one.

What is FLUTD and how is it treated?

An umbrella term for feline lower urinary tract conditions. The most common in younger cats is idiopathic cystitis — stress-triggered inflammation with no bacteria present. Management: stress reduction, wet food, water fountains, environmental enrichment. Most episodes resolve within a week, but recurrence is common without addressing the triggers.

Can stress cause urinary problems in cats?

Yes — feline idiopathic cystitis is directly stress-driven in many cats. Household changes, new pets, litter box issues, and boredom are all real triggers. Managing the environment and increasing water intake are the two most effective prevention strategies.

How do I know if my dog has a UTI?

Frequent urination with small volumes, straining, blood-tinged urine, and genital licking are the classic signs. Female dogs are more prone. Diagnosis requires urinalysis and urine culture — we need both to identify the organism and choose the right antibiotic. Book an exam.

What causes crystals in cat or dog urine?

Minerals precipitate out of solution under certain pH and concentration conditions. Common types: struvite and calcium oxalate. Diet, water intake, and genetics all play roles. Urinalysis identifies crystal type, and management depends on which type — dietary change, increased water, or surgery for stones that don't dissolve.
