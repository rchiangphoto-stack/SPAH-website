Dog Urinary Issues: UTIs, Bladder Stones & Incontinence Explained

Urinary problems in dogs are extremely common — and they are also frequently misunderstood. A dog who is urinating more than usual, having accidents in the house, or producing blood-tinged urine could have anything from a simple bacterial infection to a bladder stone to a hormonal condition affecting sphincter function. Getting the right diagnosis matters, because treating the wrong problem leads to continued symptoms and potentially worsening disease.

This guide covers the most common urinary conditions in dogs, how they're diagnosed, and what you should know before your dog's veterinary appointment.

Urinary Tract Infections in Dogs

Bacterial urinary tract infections (UTIs) are among the most common conditions diagnosed in dogs. They occur when bacteria ascend into the bladder — and sometimes further, into the kidneys. Female dogs are more anatomically predisposed than males due to a shorter, wider urethra that provides less of a barrier to ascending bacteria.

Classic signs of a UTI include:

  • Frequent urination, often with only small volumes produced
  • Straining or squatting for longer than usual
  • Blood-tinged urine (hematuria) — ranging from faint pink to visibly red
  • Urinating in unusual locations or having accidents indoors in a previously reliable dog
  • Licking at the vulva or prepuce
  • Strong or unusual urine odor

Diagnosis requires a urinalysis — examining urine under a microscope for white blood cells, bacteria, and red cells. When infection is found or suspected, a urine culture and sensitivity test identifies the specific bacterial species and the antibiotics it responds to. This step is important: treating a UTI with the wrong antibiotic clears symptoms temporarily but does not eliminate the infection, leading to recurrence and potential antibiotic resistance.

A follow-up urinalysis 5–7 days after completing the antibiotic course confirms the infection has been cleared. This step is frequently skipped by owners who feel their dog has recovered — and frequently allows partially treated infections to become recurrent problems.

Recurrent UTIs Deserve Investigation

A dog who has had two or more UTIs within a year needs a deeper workup. Common underlying causes of recurrent UTIs include:

  • Bladder stones or crystals (create a rough surface where bacteria thrive)
  • Vulvar conformation issues (skin folds that harbor bacteria)
  • Underlying conditions that suppress immunity or alter urine characteristics: diabetes mellitus, Cushing's disease, hypothyroidism, or chronic steroid use
  • Bladder masses or polyps
  • Incomplete bladder emptying from neurological or anatomical causes

Abdominal imaging — X-rays or ultrasound — is a valuable next step for a dog with recurrent UTIs. Bloodwork evaluates for systemic conditions. Identifying and treating the underlying cause is the only way to break the cycle of recurrence.

Bladder Stones in Dogs

Urinary tract stones (uroliths) form when minerals in the urine precipitate and crystallize into solid masses in the bladder. They range from tiny sand-like crystals to large smooth stones. Common types include:

Struvite (Magnesium Ammonium Phosphate) Stones

Struvite is the most common bladder stone type in dogs and almost always associated with bacterial infection — specifically bacteria that produce urease, an enzyme that raises urine pH. Treating the infection and feeding a dissolution diet (prescription food formulated to dissolve struvite) can resolve these stones non-surgically over several weeks, confirmed with follow-up X-rays. Without addressing the underlying bacterial infection, the stones recur.

Calcium Oxalate Stones

Calcium oxalate stones are the second most common type in dogs and are increasing in frequency, partly due to the high prevalence of acidified diets. Unlike struvite, these stones cannot be dissolved with diet — they require surgical removal (cystotomy). Certain breeds including Miniature Schnauzers, Bichon Frises, Shih Tzus, and Lhasa Apsos are predisposed. Prevention focuses on dilute urine (encouraging water intake) and sometimes dietary modification.

Urate Stones

Urate stones are associated with liver disease (portosystemic shunts) or, in Dalmatians, a genetic metabolic trait affecting uric acid handling. They are radiolucent — invisible on standard X-rays — and require ultrasound or contrast studies for detection. Treatment involves addressing the underlying cause and dietary management.

Signs of bladder stones include recurrent UTIs, hematuria, straining, and in cases of urethral obstruction, complete inability to urinate. Diagnosis typically uses abdominal X-rays (most stones are visible; urate stones require ultrasound) and urinalysis.

Urinary Incontinence in Dogs

Urinary incontinence — involuntary urine leakage — is distinct from behavioral issues and house training problems. An incontinent dog is often unaware urine is leaking; owners typically notice wet spots where the dog was sleeping or resting, or dribbling during excitement.

Urethral Sphincter Mechanism Incompetence (USMI)

The most common cause of incontinence in adult dogs is USMI, also called hormone-responsive incontinence. It predominantly affects spayed female dogs, particularly large breeds. After spaying, declining estrogen levels weaken the urethral sphincter, reducing its ability to maintain closure during rest. It is not painful and does not affect normal voluntary urination — the dog urinates normally but leaks passively during sleep.

USMI is highly treatable. Phenylpropanolamine (PPA) is the most commonly prescribed medication and is effective in the majority of affected dogs. Diethylstilbestrol (DES) is another option. Most dogs have excellent long-term control with medication.

Other Causes of Incontinence

Other causes of urinary incontinence include:

  • Neurological disease — spinal cord injury, intervertebral disc disease, or degenerative myelopathy can impair the neural control of urination
  • Ectopic ureter — a congenital defect where one or both ureters open in an abnormal location, bypassing the urethral sphincter; diagnosed with imaging, may require surgery
  • Overflow incontinence — a chronically overfull bladder (from obstruction or neurological cause) that leaks continuously
  • Senile incontinence — older dogs may lose some sphincter control as part of aging changes

Other Urinary Conditions in Dogs

Polyuria/Polydipsia (PU/PD)

Dogs drinking excessively and urinating large volumes frequently (not just small amounts with straining) may have a systemic condition affecting urine concentration: diabetes mellitus, Cushing's disease (hyperadrenocorticism), diabetes insipidus, kidney disease, liver disease, or hypercalcemia. This is a different presentation from UTI and requires a different diagnostic approach — bloodwork, urinalysis, and sometimes hormonal testing.

Prostate Disease in Intact Male Dogs

Intact male dogs are prone to benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH) as they age — the prostate enlarges and can compress the urethra, causing straining and difficulty urinating. BPH is resolved by castration (neutering). Intact males can also develop prostatitis (bacterial infection of the prostate) or prostatic cysts, both of which affect urinary function and require veterinary management.

How Your Vet Diagnoses Urinary Problems

A thorough urinary workup at our clinic typically includes:

  • Urinalysis — evaluates urine concentration, pH, protein, glucose, blood, and sediment. A mid-stream catch or cystocentesis (bladder tap) sample is preferred for accuracy.
  • Urine culture and sensitivity — identifies specific bacteria and antibiotic sensitivities when infection is suspected
  • Abdominal X-rays — detect most bladder stones; assess prostate size in males
  • Abdominal ultrasound — evaluates bladder wall, kidneys, prostate, and detects masses or radiolucent stones
  • Blood panel — screens for systemic conditions causing urinary symptoms (diabetes, kidney disease, Cushing's, liver disease)

This comprehensive approach means your vet is not guessing — treatment follows a specific diagnosis, and outcome is far better than repeated antibiotic courses without understanding the underlying cause.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my dog has a UTI?

Signs include frequent urination with small amounts produced, straining, blood-tinged urine, licking at the genitals, and accidents indoors. Female dogs are more prone. Diagnosis requires urinalysis and ideally a urine culture — symptoms alone overlap with other urinary conditions. Book at spah.la/contact.

Can a dog UTI go away on its own?

Occasionally, but it is not reliable. Untreated infections can ascend to the kidneys (pyelonephritis), a more serious condition. If the underlying cause is a bladder stone, the infection will recur without addressing it. Proper antibiotic treatment based on urine culture is the appropriate approach.

My spayed female dog leaks urine when she sleeps — is this a UTI?

Not usually. Urethral sphincter mechanism incompetence (USMI) is common in spayed female dogs and causes passive urine leakage during rest without pain or straining. It is not a UTI and is highly responsive to medication. A vet visit can confirm the diagnosis and prescribe treatment.

What breeds are most prone to bladder stones?

Miniature Schnauzers, Bichon Frises, Shih Tzus, Yorkshire Terriers, and Lhasa Apsos are among the breeds with higher rates of bladder stones. Dalmatians have a genetic predisposition to urate stones. Breed predisposition is one reason annual urinalysis is valuable in at-risk dogs.

How are bladder stones treated in dogs?

Treatment depends on stone type. Struvite stones can often be dissolved with antibiotics and a prescription dissolution diet. Calcium oxalate and other stones require surgical removal (cystotomy). Your vet will use X-rays and urinalysis to determine which approach is appropriate and create a prevention plan to reduce recurrence.
