Guinea Pig Teeth and Diet: Why What Your Pig Eats Affects Their Whole Body

Guinea pigs are popular exotic pets, but their dental and dietary needs are widely misunderstood — and the consequences of getting it wrong can be serious. Dental disease is one of the top reasons guinea pigs end up at an emergency exotic vet, and it's almost always connected to diet. If you're looking for thorough exotic care in the area, our guinea pig vet page covers what to expect at a visit.

Guinea Pig Tooth Anatomy: The Basics

Guinea pigs are true herbivores with open-rooted (elodont) teeth that grow continuously throughout their lives — all 20 of them (4 incisors, 4 premolars, and 12 molars). The incisors are visible at the front of the mouth; the cheek teeth are deep inside and impossible to examine without proper equipment and sedation or anesthesia. The entire dental arcade depends on constant, correct wear from appropriate food. When that doesn't happen, teeth overgrow, develop sharp spurs, and cause pain and injury to the tongue and cheek tissue.

What Guinea Pigs Actually Need to Eat

  • Unlimited grass hay (timothy, orchard, meadow, or blue) — this is the cornerstone of a guinea pig diet. Long-strand hay requires the lateral chewing motion that keeps cheek teeth worn correctly. It should make up 70–80% of the total diet. No amount of hay is too much.
  • Fresh leafy greens daily — romaine, red leaf lettuce, cilantro, parsley, and bell pepper are all good choices. Avoid high-oxalate greens like spinach in large amounts, as they can contribute to urinary issues over time.
  • Vitamin C supplementation — guinea pigs cannot synthesize their own vitamin C, just like humans. Deficiency causes scurvy, which presents as joint pain, poor coat quality, and immune suppression. Fresh bell pepper is one of the best natural sources; half a small pepper daily provides adequate vitamin C reliably.
  • A small amount of guinea pig pellets — not rabbit pellets, which have a different formulation and lack the vitamin C supplementation guinea pigs require. Pellets should supplement hay, not replace it. A small handful per day is appropriate; more than that often shifts the diet away from hay.
  • What to avoid: iceberg lettuce (essentially water with no meaningful nutrition), very high-calcium foods in excess (kale in large amounts), fruit (high sugar content), seeds and nuts, and any people food. A single grape is not an emergency, but fruit should not be a regular feature of the diet.

How Diet Leads to Dental Disease

A guinea pig eating primarily pellets and watery vegetables without adequate hay doesn't perform the lateral chewing action that wears cheek teeth down. Over time, the cheek teeth overgrow and develop sharp points and spurs that cut into the tongue and the inside of the cheeks. In severe cases, the tongue becomes trapped between overgrown tooth arcades — a condition called tongue trapping — which makes eating nearly impossible.

Once a guinea pig starts eating less because of dental pain, the problem accelerates rapidly. Less chewing means more overgrowth, which means more pain, which means less eating. This cycle can become life-threatening quickly because guinea pigs have very little metabolic reserve. A guinea pig that stops eating entirely can go into gastrointestinal stasis and deteriorate within 24–48 hours. Dental disease caught early is manageable; dental disease caught late can be a much harder situation.

Signs of Dental Problems in Guinea Pigs

  • Selective eating — preferring soft foods and avoiding hay is often the very first sign. A pig that used to eat hay freely and now ignores it is telling you something.
  • Drooling or a persistently wet chin — saliva pooling because swallowing is uncomfortable.
  • Weight loss — weigh your guinea pig weekly on a kitchen scale. Small ongoing losses of even a few grams per week add up fast in an animal that may only weigh 900–1,200 grams total.
  • Dropping food from the mouth while chewing (quidding) — you may notice small chewed-up wads of hay or greens on the cage floor near where the pig eats.
  • Reduced fecal output — a pig eating less makes fewer droppings. A sudden decrease in the number or size of pellets in the cage warrants attention.
  • Eye discharge — cheek tooth roots sit very close to the eye socket. Root elongation and periapical infection can cause epiphora (tearing) and discharge on the same side as the dental problem.
  • Swollen jaw or facial asymmetry — a firm or soft swelling along the jaw line can indicate a dental abscess. These require prompt veterinary attention.
  • Teeth chattering — a clicking or grinding sound made while eating can indicate that the teeth are meeting abnormally.

Diagnosis and Treatment

Diagnosing cheek tooth problems requires sedation or anesthesia — there is simply no way to examine the cheek teeth in an awake guinea pig adequately. The oral cavity is narrow, the cheek pouches are soft, and a conscious guinea pig will not tolerate the positioning needed. Dental radiographs (X-rays) are an essential part of the exam; they show tooth root elongation, bone involvement, and abscess formation that aren't visible on physical exam alone, even under anesthesia.

Treatment involves burring (filing) sharp points and spurs down to a normal occlusal surface under anesthesia. Many guinea pigs with early dental disease do very well with regular dental procedures — typically every 3–6 months — combined with meaningful diet correction (more hay, less pellets). Advanced disease, particularly elongation of the tooth roots with bone involvement or abscess formation, carries a more guarded prognosis and may require a longer treatment course. Scurvy: The Other Dietary Emergency

Vitamin C deficiency (scurvy) is the second major diet-related condition in guinea pigs, and it can develop surprisingly quickly — within weeks on a poor diet. Signs include poor coat quality, reluctance to move due to joint pain, swollen feet, and poor wound healing. Scurvy can also worsen dental disease by affecting the integrity of the periodontal ligament.

Fresh bell pepper (half a small pepper daily) provides adequate vitamin C and is highly palatable to most guinea pigs. Vitamin C added to drinking water is not a reliable supplementation method — the vitamin degrades rapidly in water, especially when exposed to light, and the actual amount ingested is unpredictable. Fresh food is always the better approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my guinea pig's teeth are okay?

The incisors you can see at the front of the mouth should be short, straight, and yellowish-orange in color — the pigmentation is normal. Incisors that appear very long, misaligned, or white may indicate a problem. However, the cheek teeth — where most dental disease actually begins — require a veterinary exam with sedation to assess properly. You cannot evaluate them at home.

My guinea pig is eating but seems to prefer pellets. Should I be concerned?

Yes — this is often the first sign of cheek tooth discomfort. A pig that avoids hay but continues to eat soft foods is telling you that chewing is uncomfortable. Pellets are soft and require much less lateral grinding to consume. Preference for pellets over hay is worth a veterinary exam, even if the pig still appears to have a normal appetite overall.

Can I give my guinea pig vitamin C drops in the water?

Not reliably. Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) degrades in water within hours, particularly when exposed to light or if the water sits in a bowl rather than a bottle. The actual amount your guinea pig ingests is largely unknown. Fresh food — particularly bell pepper — is a much more dependable source and has the added benefit of providing enrichment and fiber.

How often should guinea pigs see a vet?

Annually at minimum. Guinea pigs over 3–4 years old benefit from twice-yearly exams, as dental and systemic disease (such as ovarian cysts in intact females and kidney disease) tends to progress more quickly in older animals. Because guinea pigs mask illness well, routine exams often catch problems before they become emergencies.

The Bottom Line

Guinea pig dental and dietary problems are almost entirely preventable with the right husbandry — and almost always traceable to diet when they do occur. Unlimited grass hay, daily fresh greens with a reliable vitamin C source, and minimal reliance on pellets are the foundations of a healthy guinea pig diet and a healthy guinea pig mouth.
