New Puppy or Kitten Near Rosemead? First 30 Days at the Vet Explained

The first month with a new puppy or kitten is a whirlwind — and it's also the most important window for getting their health started on the right track. If you're in Rosemead or nearby in the San Gabriel Valley, South Pasadena Animal Hospital is about 8 minutes west on Garvey Avenue. Here's a plain-English guide to what happens at those early vet visits, why the timing matters, and what to watch for at home in between.

Why the first vet visit matters more than you might expect

New puppies and kittens often arrive with a clean bill of health from a breeder or shelter — and sometimes they have something going on that wasn't caught. Intestinal parasites are common in young animals, even from reputable sources. Congenital issues (heart murmurs, umbilical hernias, undescended testicles) are easiest to address when found early. And establishing a vaccine schedule from the correct starting point — based on your individual pet's age and what they've already received — requires a physical exam and a conversation with your vet.

The first visit is also about you. A good first appointment leaves you with a clear picture of what comes next: what vaccines are due and when, whether deworming is needed, what to feed and how much, and the signs that should send you back in between scheduled visits.

New puppy: what the first 30 days look like

Visit 1 — within the first week

The goal of the first visit is a full physical examination plus a health history review. The vet will go over whatever vaccination and deworming records came with your puppy, assess their weight and body condition, listen to heart and lungs, check for parasites or skin issues, and start building the schedule for what's next. If fecal testing is appropriate, that can be done here. This visit is about establishing a baseline and making sure nothing was missed before your puppy came home.

Puppy vaccine schedule overview

Core vaccines for puppies include the DHPP combination (distemper, hepatitis/adenovirus, parvovirus, parainfluenza) and rabies. The DHPP series is given every 3–4 weeks starting between 6 and 8 weeks of age and continues until the puppy is at least 16 weeks old — this timing is critical because maternal antibodies in the puppy's system can block the immune response to vaccines given too early. Giving the last vaccine in the series before 16 weeks risks leaving a gap in protection.

Rabies vaccination is typically given once at or after 12 weeks (or 16 weeks, depending on the product). Bordetella (kennel cough) is recommended if your puppy will attend classes, daycare, boarding, or dog parks.

Deworming

Most puppies are started on deworming at 2 weeks of age and continue on a protocol through their early weeks. Your vet will confirm the current status and prescribe additional rounds if needed. Roundworms and hookworms are common — both can be transmitted through the environment, and roundworms can pass from mother to puppy before birth or through nursing.

Spay/neuter timing

For most dogs, the general window is 4–6 months of age, before the first heat cycle for females. Larger breeds may benefit from waiting slightly longer to allow for complete musculoskeletal development — your vet will discuss the timing that's right for your dog's size and breed at their wellness visits.

New kitten: what the first 30 days look like

Visit 1 — within the first week

Same basic structure as puppies: full physical exam, review of whatever records came with the kitten, fecal check if appropriate, and a vaccine schedule built around the kitten's actual age and history. Upper respiratory infections, ear mites, and intestinal parasites are common in young kittens — all treatable when caught early.

Kitten vaccine schedule overview

Core kitten vaccines are FVRCP (feline viral rhinotracheitis, calicivirus, panleukopenia — the equivalent of distemper protection for cats) and rabies. The FVRCP series follows the same 3–4 week interval logic as DHPP in puppies, continuing until at least 16 weeks. FeLV (feline leukemia) is recommended for any kitten with potential exposure to other cats of unknown status, including future outdoor access.

Spay/neuter timing for kittens

Kittens can go into heat earlier than most people expect — as young as 4 months in some individuals. Spaying before the first heat is recommended for females; neutering around the same age is standard for males. Shelters often neuter at younger ages; if your kitten came from a rescue, they may already be altered.

Signs to call the vet before the next scheduled visit

In the first weeks at home, call us sooner if you notice:

  • Not eating or drinking for more than 12–24 hours (shorter window for kittens under 8 weeks)
  • Vomiting or diarrhea more than once, or any blood in stool or vomit
  • Lethargy that's unusual — sleeping more than normal, not interested in play or interaction
  • Sneezing or nasal discharge, especially if accompanied by eye discharge or loss of appetite
  • Any labored breathing or open-mouth breathing (emergency — call immediately)
  • Pale or white gums (emergency)
  • Limping or reluctance to use a limb

Young animals can decline quickly. When in doubt, it's always better to call and ask than to wait and see.

Questions we hear often

When should I take my new puppy to the vet for the first time?

Within the first week of bringing them home, and no later than two weeks after. Early visits establish a health baseline, confirm deworming status, and build the vaccine schedule from the right starting point.

What vaccines does my puppy need and when?

Core vaccines are DHPP (combination) and rabies. The DHPP series starts at 6–8 weeks and is given every 3–4 weeks until the puppy is at least 16 weeks old. Rabies is given once at or after 12–16 weeks. Bordetella is recommended for puppies with dog-to-dog contact.

What vaccines does my kitten need?

Core vaccines are FVRCP (series starting at 6–8 weeks, continuing every 3–4 weeks through 16 weeks) and rabies. FeLV is recommended for kittens with any potential outdoor or multi-cat exposure.

When should I get my puppy or kitten spayed or neutered?

Generally 4–6 months for most cats and smaller dogs. Larger dog breeds may benefit from waiting slightly longer. Your vet will discuss the right timing at wellness visits based on your pet's size, breed, and individual development.
