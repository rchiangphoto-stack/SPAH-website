Saying Goodbye: A Compassionate Guide to Pet Euthanasia

There is no part of loving a pet that is harder than this one. When a beloved animal is nearing the end of life — whether from old age, cancer, organ failure, or a sudden illness — families face decisions that carry enormous emotional weight. The goal of this guide is not to make that weight lighter, but to make it clearer. Understanding what quality of life assessment looks like, what the euthanasia process actually involves, and what your options are afterward can help you feel prepared — which is different from feeling ready, which may never fully come.

If your pet is ill and you have questions about end-of-life care, please reach out to our team. These conversations are ones we take seriously, and you should never feel alone in navigating them.

When Is It Time? Assessing Quality of Life

The question families ask most often is: "How do I know when it's time?" There is no precise moment where the answer becomes obvious. What most veterinarians do encourage is shifting the question slightly: instead of "when is it time to let go," ask "what does my pet's daily life feel like right now?"

A widely used framework is the HHHHHMM Quality of Life Scale, developed by veterinary oncologist Dr. Alice Villalobos. It assesses seven dimensions:

  • Hurt — Is pain adequately managed? Is your pet able to breathe comfortably?
  • Hunger — Is your pet eating enough to maintain adequate nutrition?
  • Hydration — Is your pet hydrated? Are fluids being maintained?
  • Hygiene — Can the pet be kept clean and free from pressure sores? Is there dignity in daily care?
  • Happiness — Does the pet express interest in life — interacting with family, responding to the environment, showing moments of enjoyment?
  • Mobility — Can the pet move enough to satisfy their needs? Do they need assistance for basic functions?
  • More good days than bad — When you observe your pet over the past week, which outnumber which?

When bad days consistently outpace good ones — when there are few moments of comfort, engagement, or pleasure — the scales have shifted toward suffering. That is when many families begin the conversation with their veterinarian about timing.

Some pets will tell you when it's time through gradual disengagement — they stop seeking interaction, stop looking out windows, stop responding to the things that once brought them joy. Others will be in acute distress. Both are valid circumstances.

A crucial point: acting before severe suffering begins is not the same as "giving up too soon." One of the greatest gifts veterinary medicine offers is preventing the kind of protracted suffering that occurs in humans who don't have access to this option. Making that choice proactively, before a pet enters crisis, is an act of profound love.

Preparing for the Appointment

Once you've decided, you do not need to rush — unless your pet is in acute distress. Taking a day or two to prepare can give your family time to say goodbye in whatever way is meaningful to you.

Some things families find helpful:

  • Let your pet have their favorite foods — whether or not they're on a special diet. This is not the time for dietary restrictions.
  • Spend time doing whatever your pet loves most, within their physical capability.
  • Consider whether family members — including children — want to be present or prefer to say goodbye beforehand at home.
  • Arrange for someone to drive you home afterward. The appointment is emotionally significant and you may not want to drive alone.
  • Call the clinic ahead of time to understand the logistics — arrival process, paperwork, whether you'll wait in a room, and payment arrangements. Many clinics handle payment in advance so it doesn't intrude on the moment itself.

What Actually Happens During Euthanasia

Many families find that knowing what to expect reduces anxiety — both their own and their pet's, since calm owners help keep pets calm.

The process typically unfolds as follows:

Pre-sedation (usually): Most veterinary practices — including ours — administer a sedative injection first, either intramuscularly or subcutaneously. This takes 5–10 minutes to take full effect. The pet becomes deeply relaxed, often lying down and appearing sleepy. This step ensures the pet is fully comfortable and free of any anxiety before the final step. It also means the IV placement for the final injection is done on a fully relaxed patient.

IV catheter placement: Once the pet is sedated, a small catheter is placed in a vein — usually the front leg. This is done gently and without distress in a sedated pet.

Final injection: The veterinarian administers pentobarbital sodium — a concentrated barbiturate — through the IV. It travels immediately to the heart and brain. The pet loses consciousness within seconds and cardiac activity ceases within 30 to 60 seconds. The process is fast, profoundly peaceful, and without pain or awareness.

After the injection, the vet will listen with a stethoscope to confirm the heart has stopped. Families can take as much time as they need afterward to say goodbye in the room.

You may notice your pet's eyes do not fully close, and there may be a final sigh or muscle movement after consciousness is gone — these are physiological reflexes, not responses to pain or awareness. Knowing this in advance can prevent confusion.

To Be Present or Not

There is no right answer. Many families find being present provides deep comfort — both to themselves and to their pet. The presence of a familiar person, a known smell, a gentle hand — these things matter to animals. Many pets pass more peacefully in the presence of someone they love.

Others find it too difficult, and step out after saying goodbye while the pet is sedated. This is also valid. Children should not feel pressured to be present. Adults should not be pressured in either direction. Honor what is right for your family.

If you're uncertain, speak to your veterinarian ahead of time. Understanding exactly what you will see can help you make a more informed choice.

After Your Pet Passes: Aftercare Options

Arrangements for your pet's remains are typically discussed either when scheduling the appointment or at the clinic afterward. Common options include:

  • Individual (private) cremation — your pet is cremated individually and ashes are returned in an urn. Many families keep these or scatter them in a meaningful place.
  • Communal cremation — pets are cremated together; ashes are not returned. This option is typically lower in cost.
  • Home burial — permitted in many areas but check local regulations. In the City of Alhambra and Los Angeles County, regulations govern depth and location of home burial.
  • Pet cemetery burial — available through local pet cemetery services in Southern California.

You do not need to decide immediately in the moments after the appointment. Take time to think about what feels meaningful to your family.

Grief and Finding Support

Pet loss is real grief. The bond between a person and their animal companion is genuine and deep, and the pain of losing them is proportional to that bond. It can be complicated by the fact that others — even well-meaning ones — sometimes minimize pet loss. This is worth naming: your grief is legitimate, regardless of what anyone else says.

Support resources include:

  • The AVMA Pet Loss Support page at avma.org offers resources, hotlines, and a directory of pet loss support groups
  • The UC Davis Pet Loss Support Hotline (800-565-1526) provides free counseling from trained veterinary students
  • The ASPCA Pet Loss Support resources at aspca.org/pet-care/pet-loss
  • Local grief support groups — some veterinary practices and humane societies host these in the Los Angeles area

Be patient with yourself. Grief does not follow a schedule. Some people feel relief alongside sorrow; both are normal. Some feel guilt about the timing of the decision; this is also extremely common and does not mean you made the wrong choice.

Euthanasia for Exotic Pets

The same principles of quality of life assessment apply to rabbits, guinea pigs, birds, reptiles, and other exotic companions — and the emotional weight is no different. What differs is that end-of-life care in exotic species requires a veterinarian with experience in these animals, as anatomy, physiology, and the most humane methods differ from those used in dogs and cats.

Rabbits who are in GI stasis, have inoperable cancer, or are experiencing unmanageable pain can be humanely euthanized via intravenous injection, typically administered into an ear vein. The process is similar to dogs and cats — pre-sedation followed by barbiturate injection.

Guinea pigs and small rodents may be euthanized via inhalant anesthetic overdose or intravenous/intracardiac injection under deep anesthesia, depending on the clinic's protocols and the patient's condition. These methods are humane and used according to AVMA euthanasia guidelines.

Birds are typically given a gaseous anesthetic first to ensure they are fully unconscious before any other step. The anatomy of avian veins requires experience and proper positioning.

Regardless of species, the goal is the same: a peaceful, painless passing. If your exotic companion is at end of life, contact our clinic to discuss your pet's specific situation, what to expect, and how we can support you and your pet through this time.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know when it's time to euthanize my pet?

The HHHHHMM quality of life scale — assessing Hurt, Hunger, Hydration, Hygiene, Happiness, Mobility, and More good days than bad — offers a structured way to evaluate your pet over time. When bad days consistently outnumber good ones, when your pet has few moments of comfort or engagement, the scales have shifted. Your veterinarian is a partner in this conversation — you don't have to decide alone.

Is it wrong to euthanize a pet who is still eating?

No. Appetite is one dimension of quality of life, not the only one. A pet can continue eating while experiencing significant pain, loss of function, or complete loss of engagement with life. Your vet can help you assess the full picture beyond just food intake.

What actually happens during euthanasia?

A sedative is given first to fully relax the pet. Then an IV injection of pentobarbital sodium is administered. The pet loses consciousness within seconds and cardiac activity ceases within 30–60 seconds. The process is fast, gentle, and peaceful — there is no pain or awareness.

Should I be present during my pet's euthanasia?

This is a deeply personal decision. Many owners find that being present provides comfort to their pet and allows a meaningful goodbye. Others find it too difficult. There is no right or wrong choice — honor what is right for you and your family. Talk with your vet beforehand so you know what to expect.

What are my options after my pet passes?

Options include individual cremation with ashes returned, communal cremation, home burial (check local regulations), and pet cemetery burial. You don't need to decide immediately. Our team can walk you through the options available. Reach out with any questions — we're here.
