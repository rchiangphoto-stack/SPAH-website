Does My Pet Have a Fever? How to Tell and What to Do

People feel their dog's ear and decide he has a fever. Or they press their hand to his nose and pronounce him healthy. Neither of those is a reliable test, and we get calls based on both every week. The only way to know if your pet actually has a fever is to take their temperature. The numbers below tell you what to do with what you find.

Normal Temperature Ranges for Pets

Pets run warmer than humans. A dog or cat at 98.6°F isn't running cool — they're hypothermic. Probably the most common misconception we deal with. Here's what normal looks like:

SpeciesNormal Rectal TemperatureFever Threshold
Dog101.0–102.5°F (38.3–39.2°C)>103°F (39.4°C)
Cat101.0–102.5°F (38.3–39.2°C)>103°F (39.4°C)
Rabbit101.3–104.0°F (38.5–40.0°C)>104°F (40°C)
Guinea Pig99–103.1°F (37.2–39.5°C)>103°F (39.5°C)
Bird (parrot)103–106°F (39.4–41.1°C)Varies; consult vet

A dog or cat at 103°F is already febrile. Don't wait for them to hit 104° before acting.

Before You Check the Temperature

None of these confirm a fever — that's what the thermometer is for. But these are what we hear about on calls and what push us toward "come in sooner rather than later":

  • Warm ears — not just from sleeping, but meaningfully hotter than their normal baseline
  • Shivering without a cold environment — the body generating heat while trying to regulate
  • Not eating, not interested in anything — near-universal with real fever across all species
  • Panting at rest — normal during exercise; not normal sitting still. Any panting in a resting cat is never normal
  • Red or glassy eyes, stopped grooming — particularly telling in cats, who groom constantly when well

The wet-nose myth, by the way, is just that — a myth. Dry nose doesn't mean fever. Hot ears plus lethargy plus not eating means take the temperature.

Taking the Temperature at Home

Rectal thermometry is the only home method that actually works. You need a digital thermometer kept solely for pet use, some lubricant, and — ideally — a second person. Here's how:

  1. Lubricate the tip well.
  2. Your helper holds the pet — one hand under the chest, one steadying the hindquarters.
  3. Lift the tail. Insert about 1 inch for dogs and cats, a bit less for small animals.
  4. Wait for the beep. Read the number.
  5. Reward the pet and disinfect the thermometer.

103°F in a dog or cat — call us today. 104°F — come in today, don't wait. 106°F — emergency clinic right now. That temperature range causes brain and organ damage and we're not being dramatic about that.

Human forehead thermometers: wrong calibration, useless for animals. Vet-grade ear thermometers are closer but placement-dependent. Rectal is the standard for a reason.

What’s Behind the Fever

Infections — the most common cause, by a large margin

Bacterial and viral infections cover most of what we see. Respiratory infections, UTIs, infected wounds, leptospirosis from contaminated standing water. Parvovirus in unvaccinated dogs is still something we hospitalize — high fever, hemorrhagic GI disease, often fatal without rapid IV support. Staying current on vaccinations meaningfully reduces the likelihood of several of the most serious fever-causing diseases.

Tick-Borne Disease

Real concern in the SGV foothill areas year-round. Dogs hiking Eaton Canyon, Millard, the San Gabriel Mountain trails — we run tick panels on these dogs when fever is unexplained and outdoor exposure is confirmed. Anaplasmosis and ehrlichiosis can cause weeks of intermittent fever, lethargy, reduced appetite — sometimes the owners have been managing it with "he's just tired lately" before connecting the dots. If your dog is a trail dog and develops unexplained fever, tick disease is near the top of our list.

Immune-Mediated Conditions

When the immune system attacks the body's own tissues — hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, polyarthritis — inflammation drives fever. These don't respond to antibiotics no matter how many courses you give. They need specific diagnostics and targeted management. Not common, but not rare. If infection gets ruled out and the fever persists, this is where we look next.

After Vaccination

Low-grade fever under 103°F in the 12–24 hours post-vaccination is expected. It means the immune system is doing its job. Pet may be quieter than usual for a day. That's fine. What we want to hear about: high fever, facial swelling, vomiting, or breathing difficulty in the hours after vaccination. That's a more significant reaction, not just the normal post-vaccine response.

Toxins

Fever as part of a toxic reaction. Xylitol (sugar-free gum, some peanut butters, many packaged foods), certain plants, some medications. If there's any chance your pet got into something and they're febrile — don't watch it develop. Call us or ASPCA Poison Control at 888-426-4435 immediately. Timing matters with toxin cases.

Fever in Exotic Pets

Rabbits above 104°F are in serious trouble — hunching, rapid shallow breathing, not moving. Fever in rabbits usually points to Pasteurella infection, an abscess, or GI disease compounding something else. They deteriorate fast. We want to see them the same day.

Guinea pigs present with marked lethargy and labored breathing. Upper respiratory infections are common and move quickly to pneumonia. A guinea pig that "just seems a little off" can tip into crisis within 24 hours. We're not exaggerating when we say these animals need prompt evaluation.

Birds: forget trying to measure temperature yourself. Watch behavior. Fluffed feathers, closed eyes during the day, sitting at the bottom of the cage, silence from a normally vocal bird — any of those warrants immediate contact with an avian-knowledgeable vet. Birds that look sick are often very sick. Hours matter, not days.

Reptiles don't generate fevers the way mammals do — they're ectothermic. But infected reptiles show behavioral fever, seeking heat obsessively. A lizard or snake pressing against the heat source constantly, or not moving even at proper enclosure temperatures — that's worth a vet visit, not a thermostat adjustment.

When to Act

  • 103–104°F: Call us today. Same-day appointment if possible.
  • Above 104°F: Come in today. Call ahead so we're ready for you.
  • Above 106°F: Emergency clinic. Now. Not tomorrow morning. Now.
  • Any fever in a puppy, kitten, rabbit, bird, or guinea pig: Urgent. Call immediately.
  • Fever plus labored breathing, pale gums, collapse, or seizures: Emergency, regardless of the number on the thermometer.

Don't try to cool a febrile pet with ice baths or cold water poured over them. Rapid cooling causes dangerous physiological shifts. We handle temperature reduction once we know the cause and can do it in a controlled way.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the normal body temperature for a dog or cat?

101.0–102.5°F for both. 103°F is already a fever. 104°F warrants a same-day visit. 106°F is an emergency — neurological damage can occur at that level. Keep a pet thermometer at home so you have a real number when you call us, not a guess based on how the nose feels.

Can I use a forehead or ear thermometer on my pet?

Forehead thermometers won't give you an accurate number — wrong calibration entirely. Veterinary ear thermometers are closer but still technique-dependent. A rectal digital thermometer is what works. Label it for pet use, keep it with their supplies.

Should I give my pet Tylenol or aspirin for a fever?

No. Hard no. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is hepatotoxic in cats — small doses cause liver failure. It's dangerous in dogs too. Aspirin causes GI bleeding and ulcers in pets. Human pain relievers are not safe for animals. Call us before giving anything, including "natural" supplements. The right treatment depends entirely on the cause of the fever.

What causes fever in pets?

Infections are the most common cause — bacterial and viral. Tick-borne disease is on our list for dogs with outdoor exposure in the SGV. Immune-mediated conditions show up when infection is ruled out. Toxin ingestion is less common but serious. Post-vaccine low-grade fever is normal and expected. When the cause isn't obvious after a standard workup, we call it fever of unknown origin and dig further — bloodwork, cultures, imaging, sometimes specialist referral.

When does a pet's fever become a medical emergency?

104°F — come in today, don't wait for tomorrow. 106°F — emergency clinic right now. Fever with labored breathing, seizures, collapse, pale or blue gums — emergency regardless of the temperature. If you're asking yourself whether to go, go. Call us on the way.
