Does My Pet Have a Fever? How to Tell and What to Do

People feel their dog's ear and decide he has a fever. Or they press their hand to his nose and pronounce him healthy. Neither of those is a reliable test, and we get calls based on both every week. The only way to know if your pet actually has a fever is to take their temperature. The numbers below tell you what to do with what you find.

Normal Temperature Ranges for Pets

Pets run warmer than humans. A dog or cat at 98.6°F isn't running cool — they're hypothermic. This is one of the most common misconceptions we encounter. Here's what normal actually looks like:

SpeciesNormal Rectal TemperatureFever Threshold
Dog101.0–102.5°F (38.3–39.2°C)>103°F (39.4°C)
Cat101.0–102.5°F (38.3–39.2°C)>103°F (39.4°C)
Rabbit101.3–104.0°F (38.5–40.0°C)>104°F (40°C)
Guinea Pig99–103.1°F (37.2–39.5°C)>103°F (39.5°C)
Bird (parrot)103–106°F (39.4–41.1°C)Varies; consult vet

A dog or cat at 103°F is already febrile. Don't wait for them to hit 104° before acting.

Signs That Suggest a Fever Before You Check

These don't confirm a fever, but they're worth noting:

  • Warm or dry nose — the wet-nose myth is wrong, but a notably hot nose alongside other signs is meaningful
  • Warm ears — significantly warmer than their usual baseline
  • Shivering or trembling — the body generating heat while trying to regulate temperature
  • Lethargy and reduced appetite — almost universal with fever across all species
  • Rapid breathing at rest — panting in dogs during activity is normal; at rest it's not. Any panting in a resting cat is not normal
  • Red or glassy eyes
  • Stopped grooming — especially telling in cats

None of these alone tells you anything definitive. The thermometer does.

How to Take Your Pet’s Temperature at Home

Rectal thermometry is the only reliable home method. You need a digital thermometer dedicated to pet use, petroleum jelly or water-based lubricant, and ideally a second person to help hold the pet still.

  1. Lubricate the tip generously.
  2. Have a helper restrain the pet — one hand under the chest, one supporting the hindquarters.
  3. Lift the tail gently and insert about 1 inch for dogs and cats, slightly less for small animals.
  4. Wait for the beep, then read the number.
  5. Reward the pet and disinfect the thermometer.

Above 103°F in a dog or cat — call us. Above 104°F — come in today. Above 106°F — emergency clinic, now. That temperature range causes brain and organ damage.

Human forehead thermometers don't work on animals. The calibration is wrong. Veterinary ear thermometers can work but are less reliable than rectal — placement technique matters more than most people realize.

What's Usually Causing It

Infections

The most common cause. Bacterial, viral — respiratory infections, UTIs, infected wounds, leptospirosis (transmitted through contaminated water). Parvovirus in unvaccinated dogs causes high fever with severe GI symptoms and is a genuine emergency. Up-to-date vaccinations meaningfully reduce the risk of several major fever-causing diseases.

Tick-Borne Disease

Year-round concern in the SGV foothills. We run tick panels on dogs with unexplained fever and outdoor exposure regularly. Anaplasmosis, ehrlichiosis, and Rocky Mountain spotted fever all cause fever, lethargy, and appetite loss — sometimes for weeks. If your dog hikes the local trails and develops unexplained fever, this is on our list.

Immune-Mediated Conditions

When the immune system turns against the body's own tissues — immune-mediated hemolytic anemia, thrombocytopenia, polyarthritis — the resulting inflammation drives fever. These require specific diagnostics and targeted management. They're not rare, and they don't respond to antibiotics.

Vaccine Reactions

A mild low-grade fever under 103°F in the 12–24 hours after vaccination is normal and expected — it means the immune system is responding. The pet may be quieter than usual for a day. That resolves on its own. High fever, facial swelling, vomiting, or difficulty breathing after vaccination is a more significant reaction and needs a call to us.

Toxins

Fever can be part of a systemic toxic reaction — certain plants, xylitol (in sugar-free gum and food), some medications. If there's any chance of ingestion and your pet is febrile, call us immediately or ASPCA Poison Control at 888-426-4435. Don't wait to see how it develops.

Fever in Exotic Pets

Rabbits above 104°F are genuinely unwell — hunching, rapid breathing, not moving. Fever in rabbits is often bacterial (Pasteurella is common), an abscess, or GI disease. These animals deteriorate quickly and need to be seen urgently.

Guinea pigs with fever show marked lethargy and labored breathing. Upper respiratory infections are common. Guinea pigs are deceptively fragile — what looks like a "mild cold" can turn into pneumonia fast.

Birds are complicated because their normal temperature ranges are wider. Don't try to measure it yourself. The clinical signs matter more: fluffed feathers, closed eyes during the day, silence when they're usually vocal. Any of those warrants immediate veterinary contact — birds that look sick are usually quite ill.

Reptiles don't run fevers the mammalian way. They're ectothermic — environmental temperature controls body temperature. But sick reptiles often seek heat (behavioral fever). If your lizard or snake is pressing against the heat source constantly or behaving unusually, infection may be driving it. Worth a vet visit.

What Temperature Means What Action

  • 103–104°F: Call us today. Same-day appointment if possible.
  • Above 104°F: Come in today even without an appointment. Call ahead so we're ready.
  • Above 106°F: Emergency clinic. Now. Brain damage occurs at this range.
  • Any fever in a puppy, kitten, or exotic pet: Urgent. Call immediately.
  • Fever plus breathing difficulty, collapse, pale gums, or seizures: Emergency without delay.

Don't try to cool a febrile pet with ice baths or cold water on your own. Rapid temperature drops cause dangerous physiological shifts. We manage temperature reduction once we know what's causing the fever and can do it safely.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the normal body temperature for a dog or cat?

101.0–102.5°F (38.3–39.2°C) for both dogs and cats. Above 103°F is a fever. Above 104°F warrants a prompt vet visit. Above 106°F is a medical emergency.

Can I use a forehead or ear thermometer on my pet?

Human forehead thermometers aren't calibrated for animals — they'll give you a wrong reading. Veterinary ear thermometers can work but are less reliable than rectal. A dedicated digital rectal thermometer is what we use and recommend for home checks.

Should I give my pet Tylenol or aspirin for a fever?

No. Acetaminophen (Tylenol) is highly toxic to cats and causes liver failure. Dangerous to dogs as well. Aspirin causes GI ulcers in pets. Call us before giving anything — the right treatment depends on the cause of the fever.

What causes fever in pets?

Most commonly infections, tick-borne diseases, immune-mediated conditions, and toxin ingestion. Post-vaccine low-grade fever is also normal. Fever of unknown origin does occur and warrants a thorough workup at our Alhambra clinic.

When does a pet's fever become a medical emergency?

Above 104°F — come in today. Above 106°F — emergency now. At that level you're looking at potential brain damage and organ failure. Fever with difficulty breathing, seizures, collapse, or pale gums: emergency clinic without delay.
