Why Is My Pet So Tired? Understanding Lethargy in Dogs, Cats & Exotic Pets

Lethargy is one of those words that gets thrown around a lot, but when owners describe it in the exam room, they're usually talking about something real and specific. The dog who ignored his food this morning. The cat who hasn't left the corner of the couch in two days. The rabbit sitting hunched in the back of the enclosure.

Sometimes that's nothing. Sometimes it's the first sign of something serious. The difference usually comes down to how long it's been going on and what else is happening alongside it.

Tired vs. Lethargic — It's Not the Same

Tiredness follows a cause. A dog who hiked five miles with you on Saturday naps the rest of the day. That's expected. True lethargy has no obvious explanation — or it persists long after the pet should have recovered.

What we mean when we say a pet is lethargic:

  • Less interest in things they normally enjoy — walks, play, greeting people
  • Sleeping more than their usual amount, or not rousing when they'd normally be awake
  • Slow to respond to their name, to food being prepared, to the usual cues
  • Quieter than normal — especially significant in a pet who's usually vocal

You know your pet's baseline. A sudden shift from that, with no obvious physical reason, is the signal worth paying attention to.

What Usually Causes It

Infections

The immune system working hard suppresses energy — bacterial, viral, and fungal infections all do this. In the SGV foothills, tick-borne disease is a real consideration. We run tick panels on dogs with unexplained lethargy and any outdoor exposure, regularly. Anaplasmosis and ehrlichiosis can cause weeks of intermittent lethargy, reduced appetite, and low-grade fever before owners connect the symptoms. A lot of "he's just been tired lately" presentations in trail-hiking dogs end up being tick-borne.

Pain

Underappreciated as a cause of lethargy. Dental disease, orthopedic problems, urinary pain, abdominal discomfort — these all show up as lethargy before they show up as obvious pain. Cats especially. A cat we'd describe as "quieter lately" may have significant pain and be managing it by staying still and disengaging from everything. Every lethargic cat we examine gets an abdominal palpation and a dental check, because those two things catch a lot of hidden pain.

Metabolic and Hormonal Conditions

Lethargy is the leading sign in several endocrine diseases before anything else becomes obvious. Hypothyroidism in dogs: profound lethargy, weight gain, cold intolerance, sometimes a slow heart rate. Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency): waxes and wanes, sometimes called "the great pretender" because it mimics so many things. Kidney disease: toxin accumulation makes animals feel genuinely awful — we catch this most in older cats. Liver disease works the same way. Uncontrolled diabetes causes fatigue and malaise alongside other signs. Blood panels sort most of this out fairly quickly.

Heart Disease

Reduced cardiac output means less oxygen reaching tissues. Early heart disease shows as exercise intolerance — tiring quickly, lagging on walks. Later: struggling to breathe. Cavalier King Charles Spaniels and Dobermans have well-documented cardiac predispositions. A middle-aged Cavalier who's "just slowing down" warrants a cardiac exam, not an assumption about aging.

Anemia

Low red cell counts mean less oxygen circulating. The gums will tell you quickly — pale, white, or gray gums in a lethargic pet are an emergency. Don't wait for an appointment. Come in or go to a 24-hour clinic. White gums in a lethargic pet: go now.

Toxin Ingestion

Lethargy is often the first sign before more obvious toxic signs appear. Rodenticides, xylitol, certain plants, some medications. If there's any possibility your pet got into something and they're acting off — call us or call ASPCA Poison Control at 888-426-4435 immediately. Don't watch it develop.

These Are Emergencies — Go Now

If lethargy comes with any of these, don't wait:

  • Pale, white, blue, or yellow-tinged gums
  • Labored breathing or fast shallow breaths
  • Collapse or can't stand
  • Seizures or disorientation
  • Distended or painful abdomen
  • Known or suspected toxin exposure
  • No urination in 12+ hours
  • Any puppy or kitten under 16 weeks — they deteriorate within hours, not days

Why Cats Mask It So Effectively

Cats are the hardest animal we treat when it comes to reading illness early. They evolved to hide weakness — prey behavior — and they're very good at it. A sick cat may keep eating (a bit less), keep grooming (less thoroughly), and appear acceptable to an attentive owner until the disease is significantly advanced. By the time the lethargy is obvious, it's often not early-stage disease anymore.

This is the practical argument for annual wellness bloodwork in cats over seven. Kidney disease and hyperthyroidism in particular — we catch these early on bloodwork long before you'd see a sign at home. The cat feels bad for months before you notice. The bloodwork tells us before that.

Rabbits, Guinea Pigs, Birds — Treat Lethargy as Urgent

Rabbit owners specifically: a hunched rabbit that isn't eating or hasn't produced droppings in 6–8 hours — that's GI stasis until proven otherwise. The gut has slowed or stopped. Fatal within 24–48 hours without treatment. Don't wait to see if it improves overnight.

Guinea pigs hide illness similarly. Lethargic, hunched, not moving around the cage — dental disease, respiratory infection, urinary stones, and vitamin C deficiency are all on the list. They can't synthesize vitamin C and scurvy develops faster than most owners expect on a diet without enough fresh vegetables.

A fluffed bird sitting at the bottom of the cage is a bird in trouble. Fluffing is a heat-conservation behavior — a sign the body is fighting something. Hours matter, not days. Bacterial infections, heavy metal toxicity, respiratory disease — any of these. An avian-knowledgeable vet, same day.

Reptiles: activity levels vary with temperature, which complicates reading lethargy. The question is whether the behavior makes sense given the current enclosure temperatures. A bearded dragon at 95°F basking temperature who's not moving and not eating — that's not temperature-related. Vet visit. Metabolic bone disease, parasites, respiratory infection, and egg binding in females are all possibilities we check for.

What We Do When You Come In

History first — when you noticed the change, eating and drinking, any recent exposures or events. Physical exam: temperature, heart rate, lung sounds, gum color, abdominal palpation, lymph nodes. We're looking for pain responses, swelling, anything abnormal.

For most lethargic pets, we start with a minimum database: CBC, chemistry panel, urinalysis. Three tests that screen the most common causes and usually either identify the problem or meaningfully narrow the list. From there, we add X-rays, ultrasound, thyroid testing, tick panels — depending on what we find and what we're still ruling out.

Most causes of lethargy are treatable when caught before they've been going on for a long time. Waiting to see if it gets better on its own often means we're starting treatment at a more advanced stage.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my pet is lethargic or just tired?

Tiredness has an explanation — a long hike, hot weather, hard play. True lethargy doesn't, or it persists well past when a tired pet would have recovered. If your pet is quieter than their normal baseline for no clear reason, that's the signal. More than 24 hours without improvement: call us.

How long should I wait before calling the vet?

Healthy adult dog or cat: 24 hours of unexplained lethargy without improvement is the threshold. Sooner if anything else is off. For puppies, kittens, rabbits, birds, guinea pigs — even a few hours warrants a call. Small animals deteriorate much faster than dogs and cats.

My dog sleeps a lot — is that normal?

Dogs sleep 12–14 hours a day typically, more for puppies and seniors. The question is whether the amount has changed from their baseline. A dog sleeping more than usual who's also slower to get excited about food, walks, or familiar people — that's a meaningful change, not just age.

What blood tests does the vet run for a lethargic pet?

CBC to check for anemia, active infection, or inflammation. Chemistry panel for kidney and liver function, blood glucose, electrolytes. Urinalysis. Those three tests together screen most common causes. Thyroid testing added for cats over seven and dogs with compatible signs. Tick panel for dogs with outdoor foothill exposure here in the SGV.

Can stress or anxiety cause lethargy in pets?

Yes — major household changes, moves, new animals, loud disruptions can cause temporary behavioral withdrawal that looks like lethargy. But stress-related quietness usually resolves within a few days. We don't attribute persistent lethargy to stress without ruling out medical causes first — stress is a diagnosis of exclusion. Book an appointment if you're unsure.
