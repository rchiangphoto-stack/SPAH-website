My Pet Is Limping: What It Means and When It’s an Emergency

Limping calls are some of the most common we get. And they cover an enormous range — from a dog with a foxtail wedged between his toes to a lab with a ruptured cruciate ligament to a cat who stepped on something hot. Figuring out where on that spectrum you are is the whole question.

Here's what we look at, and how to tell whether you need an appointment this week or right now.

What's Usually Causing the Limp

Something in the Paw

This is probably the most common cause of sudden limping in dogs who've been outside — and the most often missed by owners who go straight to "it must be a sprain." Foxtails are a real hazard here in the San Gabriel Valley. Their barbs let them drill into paw pads, between toes, and even deep into soft tissue. We pull them from some pretty deep places.

Other things that land in paws: cuts, lacerations, broken or overgrown nails, interdigital cysts (lumps between toes), insect stings, and in summer — burns from hot asphalt. If the sidewalk is too hot for your hand, it's too hot for paws.

Soft Tissue Injuries

Strains and sprains from vigorous play, jumping, or sudden turns. These usually cause a mild to moderate sudden limp that improves with rest over a few days. But not always. Cranial cruciate ligament rupture — the dog equivalent of an ACL tear — causes sudden, significant rear leg lameness. It doesn't heal on its own. Most dogs need surgery, and the sooner it's addressed, the better the outcome for the joint.

Arthritis

Incredibly common in middle-aged and older dogs and cats. The difference from an acute injury is the onset — gradual stiffness when getting up from rest, reluctance to use stairs, slowing down on walks. Cold weather makes it worse. Cats with arthritis often just stop jumping onto furniture rather than visibly limping, which is why so many owners don't notice until it's quite advanced.

Breed-Specific Orthopedic Conditions

Luxating patella (kneecap that pops out of place) is common in small breeds — Yorkies, Chihuahuas, Pomeranians. Hip dysplasia affects Goldens, German Shepherds, Labradors. Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease shows up in small breed puppies. If you have a breed with known orthopedic tendencies and your young dog is limping, it's worth getting X-rays sooner rather than later.

Fractures

Immediate, severe, non-weight-bearing. The pet won't put the leg down at all. Often vocalizing. This is always an emergency. Don't bandage it at home — just keep the pet as still as possible and get to us immediately.

Neurological Problems

Sometimes what looks like a limp is actually a neurological issue — IVDD, spinal cord injury, or nerve damage. The clue is weakness rather than pain: knuckling (walking on the top of the paw), dragging a leg, loss of coordination. If you see any of that, urgent evaluation is needed.

Sudden vs. Gradual: It Changes Everything

Sudden, severe limping in a pet who was fine an hour ago — acute injury, fracture, ligament rupture, or something in the paw. These need prompt attention.

Gradual limping developing over weeks or months — arthritis, a developmental joint condition, or in older large-breed dogs, possibly a bone tumor. Gradual doesn't mean less serious. It means a different cause.

Intermittent — limps for a while, then seems fine — that pattern is classic for luxating patella or early cruciate disease in dogs, and arthritis flares in cats. Still warrants an exam even though it comes and goes.

When It's a Same-Day Situation

Come in today if:

  • The pet won't put any weight on the leg at all
  • There's a visible wound, exposed tissue, or significant swelling
  • The limb looks deformed or is at an abnormal angle
  • The pet is clearly in pain — vocalizing, panting, won't let you touch it
  • The limping came after a trauma — hit by a car, fall from height
  • There's knuckling, leg dragging, or loss of coordination
  • Other symptoms alongside the limp: vomiting, trouble breathing, lethargy

A same-week appointment is fine for: mild limping that doesn't worsen with rest over 24–48 hours, gradual-onset lameness in a senior pet, intermittent limping that comes and goes.

The Quick Home Paw Check

If the limp started suddenly and involves a foot, take a look before coming in. Do this calmly — a dog in pain can bite even a familiar person.

  1. Have someone hold the pet still if possible.
  2. Check each paw pad for cuts, redness, or foreign material.
  3. Spread the toes gently and look between them — foxtails hide here.
  4. Check the nails — a broken nail at the quick is painful and obvious.
  5. If you find a small visible splinter or debris and can remove it easily, do so. Anything embedded deeply — bring them in.

If the paw looks normal and the problem is in the upper leg, shoulder, or hip, don't try to manipulate the joint at home. Those structures need proper imaging to assess safely.

Limping in Exotic Pets

Exotic animals have the same urgency, just different causes.

Rabbits that stop hopping normally or drag a hind leg — think splayleg, spinal injury, or hind limb weakness. Any rabbit with rear limb problems needs to be seen promptly. They deteriorate quickly when they're in pain and can't move.

Guinea pigs are prone to vitamin C deficiency, which causes joint pain and reluctance to move. Scurvy in guinea pigs presents as a lethargic, hunched animal that doesn't want to walk. Arthritis also occurs in older animals.

Birds with foot problems — bumblefoot is the most common — show red, swollen, or crusted pads from hard perch surfaces or vitamin A deficiency. Gout causes swollen painful joints. Any bird holding a foot up or reluctant to grip its perch needs an avian-knowledgeable vet.

Reptiles without adequate UVB and calcium develop metabolic bone disease. The bones soften and fracture easily. A bearded dragon, iguana, or tortoise that can't bear weight — if their lighting and calcium supplementation isn't right — should be considered MBD until proven otherwise. Early treatment prevents permanent deformity.

What the Exam Looks Like

We watch the pet walk first. Gait, weight distribution, where the head bobs — all informative. Then we palpate every joint for swelling, pain, and range of motion, and check the spine and muscles for tenderness.

X-rays are usually needed for anything beyond a simple paw issue. Fractures, hip dysplasia, arthritis, bone tumors — they all show up on radiographs. For more ambiguous cases, joint fluid analysis distinguishes infectious from immune-mediated from degenerative disease.

Treatment depends entirely on what we find. From rest and anti-inflammatory medication for a mild sprain, to surgery for a cruciate tear or fracture. Chronic orthopedic pain is very real in pets — they compensate for it quietly, which doesn't mean they're okay. We don't accept "she's just slowing down with age" as a diagnosis without ruling out treatable causes first.

Frequently Asked Questions

My dog started limping suddenly with no obvious injury — what could have caused it?

Most commonly: something in the paw (foxtail, splinter, cut), a soft tissue strain, or a joint problem like a luxating patella or cruciate tear. Arthritis can also cause sudden-seeming lameness after a period of rest. A vet exam with X-rays identifies the cause.

Can I give my dog ibuprofen or aspirin for limping?

No. Ibuprofen and naproxen are toxic to dogs and cats — GI bleeding, kidney failure, can be fatal even in small doses. Aspirin causes GI ulcers in pets. Call us for safe veterinary pain relief options instead.

My dog is limping but still eating and playing — should I still see the vet?

Yes, within a day or two if it persists. Pets compensate well for pain and keep going even with moderate discomfort. That doesn't mean the problem is minor. A cruciate tear in a dog who's still fetching the ball is still a cruciate tear. Delaying repair allows secondary joint changes that complicate treatment.

How do vets diagnose the cause of limping?

We watch the animal move, then do a hands-on orthopedic and neurological exam — palpating joints, testing range of motion, assessing muscle mass. X-rays evaluate bone and joint structure. Joint fluid analysis is sometimes added for ambiguous cases.

Can limping in pets be a sign of cancer?

Yes, though it's not the first thing we look for. Osteosarcoma is more common in large and giant breed dogs and shows as progressive limping, often with visible swelling over the bone. Any painful, swollen limb in a large older dog warrants X-rays promptly. Book at spah.la/contact.
