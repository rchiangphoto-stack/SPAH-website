My Pet Is Limping: What It Means and When It’s an Emergency

Limping calls are some of the most common we get. And they cover an enormous range — from a dog with a foxtail wedged between his toes to a lab with a ruptured cruciate ligament to a cat who stepped on something hot. Figuring out where on that spectrum you are is the whole question.

Here's what we look at, and how to tell whether you need an appointment this week or right now.

What’s Usually Behind It

Something in the paw — check this first

Probably the most common cause of sudden limping in dogs who've been outside, and the most commonly overlooked by owners who jump straight to "must be a sprain." Foxtails are a serious hazard in the San Gabriel Valley from spring through summer — their barbs let them drill inward from paw pads, between toes, and deep into soft tissue. We pull them from places that would surprise you. Check between the toes every time a dog comes in limping after outdoor time.

Other paw culprits: cuts and lacerations, broken or cracked nails, interdigital cysts, insect stings, burns from hot asphalt. If the sidewalk is too hot for the back of your hand, it's too hot for paw pads. That rule holds in July and August around here.

Soft tissue injury

Strains and sprains usually cause a mild-to-moderate sudden limp that improves with a couple days of rest. But not always — cranial cruciate ligament rupture (dog equivalent of an ACL tear) causes sudden significant rear leg lameness and doesn't get better with rest. It doesn't heal on its own. Surgery is usually the answer, and the sooner we address it the better the joint outcome. We see dogs who were "resting it at home" for three weeks before coming in, and by then the secondary joint changes have started.

Arthritis

Very common in middle-aged and senior dogs, and more common in cats than most owners realize. The clue is the pattern — gradual stiffness getting up from rest, slowing on walks, reluctance to use stairs. Cold mornings make it worse. Cats with arthritis often just quietly stop jumping on furniture, stop climbing to their usual spots. Owners interpret that as personality change or "getting older." By the time a cat is visibly limping, the arthritis has usually been there a while.

Breed-specific joint conditions

Luxating patella — kneecap that pops out of the groove — is common in Yorkies, Chihuahuas, Pomeranians, Poodles. They'll skip a few steps mid-walk, then resume normally. Hip dysplasia runs in Goldens, German Shepherds, and Labs. Legg-Calvé-Perthes disease shows in small breed puppies, causing progressive hind leg lameness. If your dog has a breed predisposition and is limping, X-rays sooner rather than later — these progress.

Fractures and neurological problems

Fractures: immediate, severe, non-weight-bearing. The pet won't touch the leg down. Often vocalizing. Don't try to splint or wrap it at home — keep them still and come in immediately.

Neurological limping looks different from orthopedic limping. Watch for knuckling — walking on the top of the paw — dragging a limb, or loss of coordination. These are signs of nerve or spinal cord involvement, not just joint pain. IVDD in Dachshunds, Corgis, and other chondrodystrophic breeds can go from mild back pain to hind limb paralysis in hours. Don't watch that one develop.

Sudden vs. Gradual — the Onset Matters

Sudden severe limp in a previously fine pet — paw issue, fracture, ligament rupture, or acute injury. Prompt attention.

Gradual limp developing over weeks or months — arthritis, a developmental joint condition, and in older large-breed dogs, possibly bone tumor. Gradual doesn't mean less serious. It means a different category of cause and a different workup.

Intermittent limping — comes and goes, seems fine between episodes — that's often luxating patella, early cruciate disease, or arthritis flares. An exam is still worth doing even though it's not constant.

Same-Day vs. Can Wait

Come in today:

  • Not bearing any weight at all on the leg
  • Visible wound, exposed tissue, bone, or significant swelling
  • Limb at an abnormal angle or visibly deformed
  • Vocalizing in pain, panting, won't let you touch the leg
  • Limping followed a trauma — car strike, fall from height, fight with another dog
  • Knuckling, dragging, or loss of coordination anywhere in the body
  • Other symptoms alongside: vomiting, labored breathing, pale gums

Can wait a day or two: mild limp that's improving with rest, gradual-onset lameness in a senior pet, intermittent limping with normal behavior between episodes.

Check the Paw First

If the limp started suddenly after outdoor time, look at the paw before you drive over. Do this calmly — a dog in pain can bite even someone they trust completely. Have someone hold them if possible. Check each pad for cuts, redness, or foreign material. Spread the toes and look between them — foxtails are found here, not on the surface. Check the nails — a broken nail at the quick is obvious and painful. Small debris you can see and reach, remove it. Anything embedded, don't dig — bring them in.

If the paw looks normal and the problem seems to be in the upper leg, shoulder, or hip — don't manipulate the joint. Those structures need imaging to assess properly and we don't want to risk making an injury worse.

Exotic Pets

Rabbits that stop hopping normally or drag a hind limb — splayleg, spinal injury, or E. cuniculi (a parasitic infection that causes hind limb paresis). Any rabbit with rear limb weakness needs to be seen the same day. They deteriorate quickly when they can't move and are in pain.

Guinea pigs and vitamin C deficiency — scurvy causes joint pain and reluctance to move. Lethargic, hunched, doesn't want to walk. Happens faster than most owners expect on a diet without enough fresh vegetables. Older guinea pigs also get arthritis.

Birds holding a foot up, reluctant to grip a perch, or with swollen red feet — bumblefoot (pododermatitis) is the most common cause. Hard perch surfaces, obesity, vitamin A deficiency. Gout also causes joint swelling and pain. Both need an avian-knowledgeable vet.

Reptiles without adequate UVB and calcium develop metabolic bone disease — bones soften and fracture from normal activity. A bearded dragon or iguana that can't bear weight, has tremors, or has limbs that seem rubbery — check the UVB and calcium supplementation. MBD until proven otherwise. Early treatment changes outcomes significantly.

What the Exam Involves

We watch the pet move first — before we touch anything. Gait, weight distribution, where the head bobs with each step, how they carry each leg. Then a hands-on orthopedic exam: palpating every joint for effusion, crepitus, pain response, and range of motion. Spine, muscles, and nerve function checked too.

X-rays for anything beyond a simple paw issue — fractures, dysplasia, arthritis, and bone tumors all show on radiographs. For ambiguous lameness, joint fluid analysis distinguishes infectious from immune-mediated from degenerative disease. Treatment ranges from rest and NSAIDs for a mild sprain to surgical repair for a cruciate tear or fracture.

One thing we push back on: "she's just slowing down with age." Age isn't a diagnosis. Treatable joint pain is very common in older pets and they compensate for it quietly. We'd rather rule that out than accept a shrug.

Frequently Asked Questions

My dog started limping suddenly with no obvious injury — what could have caused it?

Check the paw first — foxtail, splinter, cut, or broken nail. If the paw looks normal, soft tissue strain or joint problem is next on the list. Cruciate tears and luxating patella in small breeds can look like sudden unexplained lameness. A vet exam with X-rays gives you a real answer instead of a guess.

Can I give my dog ibuprofen or aspirin for limping?

No. Ibuprofen and naproxen are toxic — GI bleeding, kidney failure, can be fatal in small doses. Aspirin causes GI ulcers. Call us for pain relief options. We have safe veterinary NSAIDs and we'd rather prescribe something appropriate than have you grab from the medicine cabinet.

My dog is limping but still eating and playing — should I still see the vet?

Yes. Pets compensate for pain well. A dog still fetching the ball can have a partial cruciate tear that needs treatment — and every week that goes by without addressing it allows the secondary joint changes to progress. "Still acting mostly normal" doesn't tell you how serious the underlying injury is.

How do vets diagnose the cause of limping?

Watch the animal move first, then orthopedic and neurological exam — palpating every joint, testing range of motion, assessing muscle mass and symmetry. X-rays for bone and joint structure. Joint fluid analysis when the picture is ambiguous. In most cases, we know the likely cause by the end of the physical exam and X-rays confirm it.

Can limping in pets be a sign of cancer?

It can. Osteosarcoma in large and giant breed dogs presents as progressive limping with swelling over a bone, most often at the distal radius or proximal tibia. Any large older dog with a painful, swollen limb that isn't responding to standard treatment warrants X-rays promptly. It's not what we hope for, but we'd rather know early. Book an appointment.
