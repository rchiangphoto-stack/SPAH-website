Pet Itching & Skin Rashes: Allergies, Parasites & What Your Vet Looks For

Skin disease is probably the category we see most often and manage most chronically at our Alhambra clinic. A dog scratching constantly could have fleas, a food allergy, a mite infestation, or a fungal infection. Each one requires a completely different treatment. Guessing — or grabbing a product off the shelf based on the packaging — delays the right diagnosis and often allows secondary infections to develop that make the original cause harder to identify.

Here's how we approach it, and what actually helps.

Why Skin Problems Are So Persistent

The skin is the body's largest organ and its first line of defense. It's exposed to allergens, parasites, bacteria, and irritants constantly. By the time most owners bring a pet in, the itching has been going on "for a while" — and the pet has scratched and licked itself into a secondary bacterial infection that now sits on top of whatever originally started the itch. That layering is what makes skin cases take more visits to fully resolve.

Our goal is to identify the primary cause — the underlying trigger — not just treat the infection on the surface. Treating only the infection without addressing what drove it leads to recurrence within weeks.

Environmental Allergies (Atopic Dermatitis)

Atopy is one of the most common dermatological diagnoses we make in dogs, and we see it in cats too. The immune system overreacts to environmental allergens — pollens, dust mites, mold spores. In the San Gabriel Valley, multiple pollen seasons overlap year-round. There's no real "off season" for allergies here, which is why so many families deal with this as a chronic management issue rather than a once-a-year problem.

In dogs, atopy typically causes recurrent itching of the paws (licking and chewing), ears, face, belly, and armpits. Skin in affected areas gets red, thickened, or darkened from chronic self-trauma. Ear infections in dogs with allergies are usually a symptom of the underlying allergy, not a standalone problem.

Cats with atopic dermatitis look different. They may show miliary dermatitis (small crusted bumps along the back), symmetrical hair loss from over-grooming, or eosinophilic plaques on the belly. Cats groom at night and out of sight — owners often notice the hair loss without realizing the cat caused it themselves.

Long-term management options include allergen immunotherapy, veterinary-prescribed anti-itch medications, medicated shampoos, and omega-3 supplementation to support the skin barrier. There's no cure — but well-managed atopy makes a huge difference in a dog's daily comfort.

Food Allergies

Food allergies are real, but they're also one of the most over-suspected diagnoses in veterinary dermatology. A true food allergy is an immune-mediated reaction to a dietary protein — usually beef, chicken, dairy, or wheat, though any protein can be the culprit.

The distinguishing feature: food allergy symptoms don't follow a seasonal pattern. Year-round itching around the face, ears, paws, and perianal area — sometimes with concurrent loose stool. That profile is worth investigating.

The only reliable diagnostic is a strict elimination diet trial: 8–12 weeks on a novel protein or hydrolyzed diet, with absolutely nothing else. No treats, no flavored medications, no dental chews. Blood and skin allergy tests for food exist, but their sensitivity and specificity in veterinary medicine are poor. We don't rely on them. The elimination trial — followed by reintroduction of the original diet to confirm recurrence — is still the gold standard.

Parasitic Skin Disease

Flea Allergy Dermatitis

The most common skin condition we see in Southern California, full stop. Fleas survive year-round in warm temperatures here. Flea allergy dermatitis isn't caused by fleas — it's caused by a reaction to flea saliva. In an allergic dog, a single bite triggers days of intense itching.

Classic presentation: intense itching and hair loss at the base of the tail, lower back, and inner thighs. "Hot spots" — areas of acute moist dermatitis — often develop. You may never see a live flea because allergic animals groom them off aggressively. Look for flea dirt: dark pepper-like specks that turn red on wet white paper.

Treatment means year-round flea control on every pet in the household plus environmental treatment. One untreated pet reinfests the whole house. Over-the-counter products vary widely in efficacy — prescription preventives work.

Sarcoptic Mange

Caused by Sarcoptes scabiei, a mite that burrows into the skin. Owners describe the itch as unlike anything they've seen in their pet — intense, relentless, affecting the ear margins, elbows, hocks, and belly. Highly contagious to other dogs; humans can get a temporary self-limiting rash. Diagnosis is difficult because skin scrapings are often negative even in active infections. We often treat empirically based on clinical picture — a treatment response confirms it.

Demodectic Mange

Demodex mites live in small numbers in the hair follicles of all normal dogs. They only cause disease when immune function is compromised. Localized demodicosis — patchy hair loss around the eyes and muzzle — is common in puppies and often resolves on its own. Generalized demodicosis is more serious, covers larger areas, and in adult dogs warrants investigation for an underlying immune-suppressing cause.

Other Common Skin Diagnoses

Ringworm is a fungal infection despite what the name implies — no worm involved. Circular, scaly patches with hair loss, sometimes with a slightly raised edge. Contagious to other animals and to humans. We diagnose with fungal culture or Wood's lamp exam. Treatment involves topical antifungals — shampoos, creams — and sometimes systemic antifungals for more widespread cases. Housemates should be checked.

Pyoderma is a bacterial skin infection and almost always sits on top of something else. Allergies creating a compromised skin barrier. A wound that didn't heal cleanly. Moisture trapped in folds or between toes. Pustules, crusting, redness, and that characteristic musty smell. We treat the infection. Then we go looking for what enabled it. Skip the second part and it's back in a few weeks.

Malassezia yeast dermatitis — greasy-feeling, musty-smelling, intensely itchy skin in the ear canals, skin folds, and between the toes. The yeast is normally present in small numbers; it blooms when the immune environment of the skin changes. Nearly always secondary to allergies, hypothyroidism, or immune conditions. Treating the yeast gives relief. Not treating the underlying driver brings it back. This is one of those cases where "treating what we can see" without investigating the root cause is a losing strategy.

Southern California-Specific Hazards

  • Foxtails — wild barley awns that embed in paws, ears, nostrils, and skin from spring through early summer. They drill inward. A sudden lump or draining tract on a pet with outdoor access should be examined — don't wait
  • Year-round fleas — prevention shouldn't stop in winter here
  • Valley fever — fungal infection from soil spores, more common further east but not absent locally. Can cause skin nodules and draining lesions alongside respiratory signs

Skin Disease in Exotic Pets

Birds losing feathers or developing bare patches — we look for feather-destructive behavior first. Over-preening or plucking driven by boredom, stress, or nutritional deficiency. Psittacine beak and feather disease (PBFD), a circovirus, also causes progressive feather loss — testing distinguishes behavioral from viral causes.

Guinea pigs get mange from Trixacarus caviae. We see this often. The itch is so intense that affected animals have seizures. Starts on shoulders and neck, spreads. Ivermectin works — but guinea pig dosing is different from general pet dosing, so don't reach for an over-the-counter formulation.

Rabbits develop fur mites (Cheyletiella — "walking dandruff") and ear mites (Psoroptes cuniculi). Both treatable, both can temporarily affect humans. Fly strike — maggot infestation of any open wound or urine-scalded area in warm weather — is an emergency if found. Rabbit skin is fragile and wounds need prompt attention.

How We Diagnose Skin Problems

We start with the most common causes in order. Flea comb first. If there's any sign of fleas, we treat immediately — you can't reliably evaluate for food allergy in a flea-allergic pet because FAD confounds all the other results.

Skin cytology identifies yeast and bacterial infections quickly. Skin scrapings look for mites. Fungal culture rules out ringworm. For complicated or recurrent cases, allergy testing and dietary trials are the next layer.

Bloodwork isn't usually the first step for skin disease — unless we're looking for hormonal causes like hypothyroidism or Cushing's that can drive secondary skin problems in older pets.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my pet has allergies vs. fleas?

Flea allergy dermatitis concentrates at the base of the tail, lower back, and inner thighs. Environmental allergies typically affect the paws, face, ears, and belly. A vet exam with skin cytology usually distinguishes them, and often both are present at the same time.

Can I use hydrocortisone cream on my dog's rash?

It provides temporary itch relief for minor irritation, but it doesn't address the cause. Don't use it on cats without veterinary guidance. And honestly — a diagnosis is more useful than a week of symptom suppression.

What is atopic dermatitis in dogs?

Chronic allergic skin disease from environmental allergens — pollens, dust mites, mold spores. Causes recurrent itching of the paws, face, ears, belly, and armpits, usually starting between 1 and 3 years of age. Managed, not cured, with immunotherapy, medications, and supportive skin care.

My cat is losing patches of fur — what could cause that?

Over-grooming from stress or allergies, ringworm, mites, hormonal imbalances, or skin infections. Most of the time in cats we discover they're grooming themselves bald at night — owners assume it's "just falling out." A skin scraping, fungal culture, or blood panel helps identify the cause. How are skin allergies diagnosed in pets?

History and physical exam first. Parasite and infection ruled out. Then for food allergy: strict 8–12 week elimination diet trial. For environmental allergy: intradermal or serum allergy testing to identify specific triggers and guide immunotherapy. Blood allergy tests for food have poor accuracy in veterinary medicine — the elimination diet is still the best tool we have.
