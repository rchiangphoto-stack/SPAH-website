Pet Itching & Skin Rashes: Allergies, Parasites & What Your Vet Looks For

Skin disease is probably what we see most chronically, week after week, at our Alhambra clinic. A dog scratching nonstop could have fleas, a food allergy, a mite infestation, or a fungal infection. Different cause, completely different treatment. The wrong call doesn't just waste time — it lets a secondary infection develop on top of the primary one, and now you have two problems instead of one.

Here's what we actually look for, and what tends to work.

Why skin problems keep coming back

By the time most owners bring a pet in, the itch has been going on "for a while." Weeks, usually. Sometimes longer. During that time, the pet has been licking, chewing, and scratching — which opens the skin barrier and creates the perfect conditions for a bacterial or yeast infection to move in. That infection now sits on top of whatever originally caused the itch.

So we're treating two things at once. The infection, which is causing real discomfort right now. And the underlying trigger — the allergy, the parasite, the hormonal imbalance — that enabled the infection. Treat only the infection and it's back in three weeks. We see this cycle constantly. The dogs who come in every few months for "another ear infection" almost always have an untreated allergy driving it.

Environmental allergies — very common in the SGV

Atopy is one of the most common things we diagnose. Dogs mostly, but cats get it too. The immune system overreacts to environmental allergens — pollens, dust mites, mold spores. Here in the San Gabriel Valley, multiple pollen seasons stack on top of each other year-round. There's no real off-season for allergies in Southern California. Owners who move here from elsewhere are often surprised: "her allergies used to clear up in the fall." Not here.

In dogs, atopy looks like recurrent itching of the paws (licking and chewing between the toes), ears, face, belly, and armpits. Skin in these areas gets red, thickened, or darkened from chronic self-trauma. The ear infections — we need to say this clearly — are usually a symptom of the underlying allergy, not a standalone problem. Treating the ear without addressing the allergy is temporary relief at best.

Cats with atopic dermatitis look completely different from dogs. Miliary dermatitis: small crusted bumps along the back. Symmetrical hair loss from over-grooming, often on the belly and inner thighs. Eosinophilic plaques. Cats groom out of sight, usually at night — owners notice the hair loss without understanding that the cat is pulling it out themselves. We see this misinterpreted as "losing hair" when it's really "removing hair."

There's no cure for atopy — that's just honest. But well-managed environmental allergies make an enormous difference in a pet's daily comfort. Options include allergen immunotherapy, prescription anti-itch medications, medicated shampoos, and omega-3 supplementation to support the skin barrier.

Food allergies — real but over-suspected

Food allergies exist and we do diagnose them. They're also one of the most over-suspected things in veterinary dermatology. A true food allergy is an immune-mediated reaction to a specific dietary protein — beef, chicken, and dairy are the most common culprits, but any protein can be the trigger.

The distinguishing feature: food allergy doesn't follow a seasonal pattern. Year-round itching around the face, ears, paws, and perianal area, sometimes with loose stool — that profile is worth investigating for food. If the itching reliably gets worse in spring, that's environmental, not food.

The only reliable diagnostic is a strict elimination diet trial: 8–12 weeks on a novel protein or hydrolyzed diet, with absolutely nothing else going in. No treats. No flavored medications. No dental chews. Blood and skin allergy tests for food allergies exist. Their accuracy in veterinary medicine is poor — we don't rely on them. The elimination trial, followed by reintroduction of the original diet to confirm recurrence, is the standard we still use.

Parasites — fleas first, always

Flea allergy dermatitis — the most common skin diagnosis in Southern California

Full stop. Fleas survive year-round here. Flea allergy dermatitis isn't caused by fleas — it's caused by the immune reaction to flea saliva. In an allergic dog, a single flea bite causes days of intense itching. You do not need to see fleas to have this diagnosis.

Classic presentation: intense itching and hair loss at the base of the tail, lower back, and inner thighs. Hot spots — areas of acute moist dermatitis — develop when the pet breaks the skin. You may never find a live flea. Allergic dogs groom them off aggressively. Look for flea dirt: dark pepper-like specks on the coat that turn red-brown on wet white paper. That's digested blood. That's flea dirt.

Treatment: year-round flea control on every pet in the household, plus environmental treatment. One untreated pet reinfests the house. Over-the-counter products vary widely. Prescription preventives are reliably effective.

Sarcoptic mange — the itch that doesn't quit

Caused by Sarcoptes scabiei, a mite that burrows into the skin. The itch is severe — owners describe it as unlike anything they've seen in their pet. Ear margins, elbows, hocks, and belly are the classic locations. Highly contagious to other dogs; humans can get a temporary rash. Diagnosis is hard because skin scrapings are often negative even in active infections. We frequently treat empirically based on clinical picture and let the treatment response confirm it.

Demodectic mange — immune-mediated, not contagious

Demodex mites are present in small numbers in the hair follicles of all normal dogs. They only cause disease when immune function is suppressed or immature. Localized demodicosis — patchy hair loss around the eyes and muzzle in young dogs — often resolves on its own. Generalized demodicosis is more serious, and in adult dogs it warrants looking for something suppressing the immune system.

Three more diagnoses worth knowing

Ringworm is a fungal infection — no worm, despite the name. Circular, scaly patches with hair loss, sometimes with a raised edge. Contagious to other animals and to people. We diagnose with fungal culture or Wood's lamp. Treatment: topical antifungals for limited cases, systemic antifungals for more widespread ones. Every pet in the house should be checked.

Pyoderma is a bacterial skin infection and it almost always lives on top of something else. Allergy. A wound that didn't heal. Moisture trapped in skin folds. Pustules, crusting, redness, that musty smell. We treat the infection — but then we go looking for what allowed it to establish. Skip that step and it's back.

Malassezia yeast dermatitis — greasy, musty, intensely itchy, usually in the ear canals, skin folds, and between the toes. The yeast is normally present in small numbers and becomes a problem when the skin environment changes. Nearly always secondary to allergies, hypothyroidism, or another immune-altering condition. Treating the yeast alone buys a few weeks. The underlying driver brings it right back. We are not going to keep treating the surface problem without diagnosing what's underneath it.

Local hazards worth knowing

  • Foxtails — wild barley awns that embed in paws, ears, nostrils, and skin from spring through early summer. They drill inward and don't come out on their own. A sudden lump, draining tract, or persistent foot-licking on a pet with outdoor access: don't wait, get it examined.
  • Year-round fleas — winter doesn't kill them here. Flea prevention shouldn't stop in November.
  • Valley fever — fungal infection from soil spores, more common further east but not rare locally. Skin nodules and draining lesions alongside respiratory signs — think about this in a dog with outdoor desert or foothill exposure.

Exotic animals and skin

Birds with bare patches or feather loss: we look for feather-destructive behavior first — over-preening or plucking from boredom, stress, or nutritional deficiency. Psittacine beak and feather disease (PBFD) is a circovirus that causes progressive feather loss. Testing distinguishes behavioral from viral. They look similar from across the room.

Guinea pigs can get mange from Trixacarus caviae. We see this. The itch is intense enough to cause seizures — that is not an exaggeration. Starts on the shoulders and neck, spreads. Ivermectin works, but guinea pig dosing is not the same as other animals. Do not reach for an over-the-counter formulation without talking to us first.

Rabbits develop fur mites (Cheyletiella — "walking dandruff") and ear mites (Psoroptes cuniculi). Both treatable. Both can temporarily affect people in the household. Fly strike — maggots in any open wound or urine-scalded skin area — is an emergency. Rabbit skin is thin and fragile. Wounds escalate fast.

How we work through a skin case

Flea comb first, every time. If there's any sign of fleas, we treat before anything else — you cannot reliably evaluate for food allergy in a flea-allergic animal because the flea allergy confounds the whole picture.

Skin cytology to identify yeast and bacteria quickly. Skin scraping for mites. Fungal culture if ringworm is on the list. For complicated or recurrent cases, allergy testing and dietary trials are the next layer.

Bloodwork isn't the first step for skin disease — unless we're looking for hypothyroidism or Cushing's, which both drive secondary skin problems in older dogs and can look like "just allergies" for months before someone checks the thyroid.

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my pet has allergies vs. fleas?

Flea allergy dermatitis concentrates at the base of the tail, lower back, and inner thighs. Environmental allergies typically affect the paws, face, ears, and belly. A vet exam with skin cytology usually distinguishes them, and often both are present at the same time.

Can I use hydrocortisone cream on my dog's rash?

It provides temporary itch relief for minor irritation, but it doesn't address the cause. Don't use it on cats without veterinary guidance. And honestly — a diagnosis is more useful than a week of symptom suppression.

What is atopic dermatitis in dogs?

Chronic allergic skin disease from environmental allergens — pollens, dust mites, mold spores. Causes recurrent itching of the paws, face, ears, belly, and armpits, usually starting between 1 and 3 years of age. Managed, not cured, with immunotherapy, medications, and supportive skin care.

My cat is losing patches of fur — what could cause that?

Over-grooming from stress or allergies, ringworm, mites, hormonal imbalances, or skin infections. Most of the time in cats we discover they're grooming themselves bald at night — owners assume it's "just falling out." A skin scraping, fungal culture, or blood panel helps identify the cause. How are skin allergies diagnosed in pets?

History and physical exam first. Parasite and infection ruled out. Then for food allergy: strict 8–12 week elimination diet trial. For environmental allergy: intradermal or serum allergy testing to identify specific triggers and guide immunotherapy. Blood allergy tests for food have poor accuracy in veterinary medicine — the elimination diet is still the best tool we have.
