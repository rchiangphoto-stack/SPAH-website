Why Is My Dog or Cat Vomiting? Causes, Warning Signs & When to See a Vet

Vomiting is one of the most common reasons pet owners call a veterinary clinic. In many cases it's a minor episode — a dog who ate too fast or a cat who hacked up a hairball — and the pet bounces back quickly. But vomiting and diarrhea can also signal something serious: a foreign body, organ disease, pancreatitis, or a toxin ingestion. Knowing how to tell the difference is one of the most useful things a pet owner can learn.

This guide covers the most common causes of vomiting and diarrhea in dogs and cats, the red flags that mean "go now," and what to expect when you bring your pet in for a GI-related exam at our Alhambra clinic.

Vomiting vs. Regurgitation: Why the Distinction Matters

Before assuming your pet is "vomiting," it's worth understanding whether what you're seeing is actually vomiting or regurgitation. They look similar but have very different causes.

Vomiting is an active process. You'll see the pet heaving, using abdominal muscles, and the material that comes up is partially digested — it may contain bile (yellow-green fluid) or stomach contents with a sour smell.

Regurgitation is passive. Food or liquid simply flows back up with no warning, often moments after eating. The material looks like undigested kibble, often in a tube or cylinder shape. Regurgitation can indicate a problem with the esophagus — such as megaesophagus, a stricture, or in some cases, a mass — and always warrants a veterinary evaluation.

When you call the clinic, describe what you saw as precisely as possible. Was there active retching? What did the material look like? How long after eating did it happen? These details help the veterinarian triage the call accurately.

Common Causes of Vomiting in Dogs and Cats

Most acute (sudden) vomiting falls into a handful of categories:

Dietary Indiscretion

Dogs are notorious for eating things they shouldn't — garbage, dead animals, table scraps, grass. This typically causes a self-limiting bout of vomiting that resolves within 12–24 hours with supportive care. Cats are generally more selective, but sudden diet changes or access to rich or spoiled food can have the same effect.

Gastritis and Gastroenteritis

Inflammation of the stomach lining (gastritis) or both stomach and intestine (gastroenteritis) is a common cause of vomiting and diarrhea together. It can be triggered by infections, parasites, dietary changes, or simply stress. Most cases respond well to a bland diet and supportive care, but severe dehydration sometimes requires IV fluids.

Intestinal Parasites

Roundworms, hookworms, giardia, and coccidia can all cause vomiting and diarrhea — particularly in puppies and kittens, or pets who spend time outdoors. A fecal exam is a quick, inexpensive way to rule these out. The AVMA recommends annual fecal screening for all pets.

Pancreatitis

Inflammation of the pancreas is painful and often causes repeated vomiting, abdominal tenderness, and lethargy. It's more common in dogs and is often triggered by a high-fat meal (think holiday table scraps). Cats can develop pancreatitis as well, sometimes alongside inflammatory bowel disease and liver disease — a triad sometimes called "triaditis."

Foreign Body Ingestion

Dogs — and occasionally cats — swallow things that get stuck in the esophagus, stomach, or intestines. Socks, toys, bones, corn cobs, and string are common culprits. A foreign body obstruction causes repeated vomiting that does not resolve, often with a hunched posture and loss of appetite. This is a surgical emergency.

Systemic Disease

Kidney disease, liver disease, hyperthyroidism (in cats), diabetes, and Addison's disease can all present with vomiting. This is one reason chronic or recurrent vomiting warrants bloodwork rather than repeated symptomatic treatment.

When Vomiting Is an Emergency

Most single episodes of vomiting in an alert, active pet are not emergencies. The following situations are different — they require same-day or emergency care:

  • Vomiting blood — bright red or dark "coffee grounds" material
  • Repeated vomiting that doesn't stop after a few hours
  • Distended or painful abdomen — especially in large or deep-chested dogs (risk of bloat/GDV)
  • Known or suspected ingestion of a toxin, medication, or foreign object
  • Weakness, pale gums, or collapse alongside vomiting
  • No urination in 12+ hours with vomiting (possible urinary obstruction or kidney failure)
  • Puppy or kitten under 12 weeks vomiting more than once — young animals dehydrate rapidly

Diarrhea in Dogs and Cats: What It Tells You

Diarrhea that occurs alone (without vomiting) is usually a lower GI sign — something happening in the large intestine, small intestine, or colon. The characteristics of the stool offer clues:

  • Large volume, watery diarrhea — often small intestine origin; watch for dehydration
  • Mucus or fresh blood in stool — often large intestine (colitis); common in stressed dogs, usually responds to treatment
  • Dark, tarry stool (melena) — digested blood from the upper GI tract; requires prompt evaluation
  • Greasy, pale, foul-smelling stool — suggests malabsorption; may indicate exocrine pancreatic insufficiency (EPI) or severe intestinal disease

A single episode of loose stool in an otherwise healthy pet eating and drinking normally often resolves with a temporary bland diet (plain boiled chicken and rice). Diarrhea lasting more than 48 hours, or any diarrhea with blood or systemic signs, should be evaluated.

Vomiting and GI Symptoms in Exotic Pets

Vomiting in the traditional sense is not possible in rabbits, guinea pigs, and many rodents — they lack the stomach anatomy and musculature to bring food back up. This is critically important: if these animals eat something toxic or develop a GI obstruction, they cannot vomit it out. GI stasis in rabbits (the gut slowing or stopping) causes bloating, discomfort, and tooth-grinding and is a life-threatening emergency requiring immediate veterinary care.

Birds can regurgitate or vomit, and the distinction matters here too. Regurgitation is a normal bonding behavior in birds (they feed each other). True vomiting in a bird — where the head shakes and material is ejected — suggests illness and should be evaluated urgently. Parrots, cockatiels, and other psittacines are adept at hiding illness; by the time vomiting is visible, the bird may already be significantly unwell.

Reptiles can regurgitate food, often due to handling too soon after feeding, incorrect temperatures, respiratory infections, or internal parasites. If your bearded dragon, ball python, or turtle is regurgitating repeatedly, a veterinary exam including a fecal float and temperature check is appropriate.

What the Vet Visit Looks Like

When you bring a vomiting or diarrhea-affected pet in, the veterinarian will take a thorough history — what and when your pet last ate, any possible toxin exposures, how many episodes occurred, and the character of what was produced. A physical exam will assess hydration, abdominal pain, and gut sounds.

Depending on findings, the workup may include:

  • Fecal examination for parasites and pathogens
  • Bloodwork (CBC, chemistry panel) to screen for systemic disease
  • Abdominal X-rays to look for gas patterns, obstructions, or masses
  • Abdominal ultrasound for more detailed soft-tissue evaluation
  • Parvovirus antigen test in unvaccinated dogs

Treatment ranges from simple supportive care (anti-nausea medication, bland diet, fluids) to hospitalization and surgery, depending on the cause. Most acute GI cases — especially in otherwise healthy adult pets — resolve quickly with appropriate treatment.

Frequently Asked Questions

My dog vomited once but seems fine — should I worry?

A single episode of vomiting in an otherwise alert, eating, and active dog is usually not an emergency. Withhold food for 4–6 hours and offer small amounts of water. If vomiting does not recur and the dog is acting normally, a watch-and-wait approach is reasonable. Call your vet if vomiting returns, if your dog seems painful, or if you suspect they ate something unusual.

What is the difference between vomiting and regurgitation?

Vomiting involves active abdominal heaving and brings up partially digested stomach contents. Regurgitation is passive — food or liquid simply flows back up with no abdominal effort, and the material usually looks tubular and undigested. Regurgitation can indicate an esophageal problem and always warrants a vet visit.

Can I give my pet Pepto-Bismol at home?

Do not give Pepto-Bismol to cats — bismuth subsalicylate is toxic to them. In dogs, it is sometimes used in small amounts for mild GI upset, but it is not recommended without veterinary guidance. Many human GI remedies are unsafe for pets. Call your vet before giving any over-the-counter medication.

What does blood in my pet's vomit mean?

Bright red blood suggests active bleeding in the upper GI tract. Dark coffee-ground material indicates digested blood, which is also serious. Either finding warrants an urgent vet visit, especially if accompanied by weakness, pale gums, or repeated episodes. A foreign body, ulcer, or clotting disorder may be involved.

My cat has been vomiting every day for a week but is still eating. Is that okay?

No — chronic daily vomiting in a cat is not normal, even if appetite is maintained. Conditions like inflammatory bowel disease, hyperthyroidism, or early kidney disease can cause persistent vomiting before other signs appear.
