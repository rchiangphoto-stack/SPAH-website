Senior Cat Care in the San Gabriel Valley: What Changes After Your Cat Turns 10

Cats are famously good at hiding how they feel. A dog with a sore joint will limp noticeably; a cat with the same problem will quietly stop jumping to the counter. This instinct to mask illness — an evolutionary holdover from when showing weakness meant becoming prey — is one of the reasons senior cats need more attentive veterinary care, not less.

At South Pasadena Animal Hospital in Alhambra, we see senior cats for twice-yearly wellness exams, targeted bloodwork, and management of the chronic conditions that show up most commonly in cats over 10. Here's what you should know about caring for an older cat.

When does a cat become "senior"?

The veterinary consensus is that cats are senior around age 10–11 and geriatric at 15 and older. In human-age equivalents, a 10-year-old cat is roughly in their mid-50s. By this stage, the organ systems that have been running for a decade start showing the wear — quietly, often without symptoms that owners notice until the problem is already well-developed.

The practical implication: once your cat hits 10, once-a-year visits are no longer the gold standard. Twice-yearly exams with bloodwork become the appropriate baseline.

The most common health issues in senior cats

Hyperthyroidism

Hyperthyroidism — an overactive thyroid gland — is the most common hormonal disorder in older cats. The thyroid produces excess thyroid hormone, which drives up metabolism. Common signs include weight loss despite a good or increased appetite, increased thirst, hyperactivity or restlessness, a scruffy or unkempt coat, and occasionally vomiting or diarrhea. Some cats with hyperthyroidism seem to owners like they're just "aging well" — eating heartily, staying active — when in fact their thyroid is running too fast.

Hyperthyroidism is detectable with a simple blood test (T4 level). Treatment options include daily oral medication, a prescription iodine-restricted diet, radioactive iodine therapy, or surgical removal of the thyroid. The condition is very manageable when caught. If left untreated, the chronic high heart rate and blood pressure from hyperthyroidism cause progressive heart and kidney damage. We have a full post on cat hyperthyroidism if you want a deeper look at symptoms and treatment options.

Chronic kidney disease (CKD)

Chronic kidney disease is extremely common in cats — estimates suggest it affects the majority of cats over 15, and many cats develop early-stage CKD in their early teens. The kidneys gradually lose the ability to filter waste products from the blood, concentrate urine, and regulate fluids and electrolytes.

Early CKD has no obvious symptoms. Cats don't feel noticeably unwell until the disease has progressed significantly. The only reliable way to catch it early is bloodwork and urinalysis — specifically kidney values (BUN, creatinine, SDMA) and urine specific gravity. Early detection matters because management (dietary changes, hydration support, medications) can slow progression substantially.

Signs of more advanced CKD include increased thirst and urination, weight loss, reduced appetite, vomiting, and lethargy. If you notice any of these in your senior cat, get them in for a workup.

Dental disease

Dental disease is nearly universal in cats over 10. Years of tartar accumulation leads to gum inflammation (gingivitis), periodontal disease, and tooth resorption — a painful process where the tooth structure breaks down from the root outward. Cats rarely vocalize dental pain; they simply eat more slowly, prefer soft food, or stop eating on the side of the mouth that hurts.

A professional dental cleaning under anesthesia is the only way to assess and treat below the gumline. Pre-anesthetic bloodwork is standard for senior cats to make sure they can tolerate anesthesia safely. Many owners are surprised by how much better their older cat seems after a dental cleaning — a cat who had been subtly uncomfortable for months becomes more active and engaged afterward.

Arthritis

Arthritis (degenerative joint disease) is very common in senior cats and very commonly missed. Because cats adapt their behavior rather than limping obviously, the signs are easy to overlook: reluctance to jump to previously favorite spots, sleeping on lower surfaces, decreased grooming of the lower back and base of the tail, and a subtle change in temperament (becoming less tolerant of handling). X-rays can confirm joint changes, but a thorough physical exam and owner history usually give a clear enough picture to start pain management.

High blood pressure (hypertension)

Hypertension in cats is often secondary to hyperthyroidism or kidney disease. It can cause retinal detachment (sudden blindness), neurological symptoms, and heart enlargement. Blood pressure measurement is straightforward with a non-invasive cuff and should be part of every senior cat's routine workup, especially if they have an underlying condition associated with hypertension.

What a senior cat wellness visit looks like at SPAH

For cats 10 and older, we recommend twice-yearly visits. Each visit includes a full physical exam — weight, heart and lung sounds, abdominal palpation, dental assessment, joint mobility, coat and skin condition — along with bloodwork and urinalysis. Blood pressure measurement is added for cats with relevant risk factors.

We review any behavioral or appetite changes you've noticed at home, because subtle owner observations often point us toward the right diagnostic direction. The goal isn't just to confirm that nothing has changed since last year — it's to catch what's starting to change before it becomes a crisis.

Nutrition and weight in senior cats

Weight trends matter in older cats — both weight loss and weight gain carry meaning. Unexplained weight loss in a senior cat should prompt a vet visit even between scheduled appointments. Unintentional weight gain can signal reduced activity from arthritis or other discomfort.

Senior cats often do well with higher-protein diets that support lean muscle mass. Cats with kidney disease require phosphorus-restricted prescription diets. Cats with hyperthyroidism have specific dietary options as well. Diet should be discussed in the context of your cat's bloodwork results — not based on the "senior formula" label on a bag alone.

Questions about senior cat care in the SGV

When is a cat considered senior?

Cats are considered senior around age 10–11. By this stage, conditions like hyperthyroidism, kidney disease, and dental disease become more common — often without obvious symptoms. Twice-yearly vet visits with bloodwork are recommended starting at age 10.

What health problems are most common in senior cats?

Hyperthyroidism, chronic kidney disease, dental disease, arthritis, and high blood pressure are the most common conditions in cats over 10. Many develop gradually and are best caught through routine bloodwork before obvious symptoms appear.

How often should a senior cat see the vet?

Twice yearly for cats 10 and older. The 6-month interval allows for bloodwork monitoring that catches slowly developing conditions before they're advanced.

What bloodwork does a senior cat need?

A complete blood count (CBC), chemistry panel (including kidney values and liver enzymes), thyroid hormone (T4), and urinalysis. Blood pressure measurement is also recommended, especially for cats with hyperthyroidism or kidney disease.
