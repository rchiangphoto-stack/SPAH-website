Signs Your Bearded Dragon Needs to See a Vet

Bearded dragons are hardy, resilient reptiles — but they're also very good at hiding when something is wrong. In the wild, showing weakness makes an animal vulnerable to predators, and that instinct doesn't go away in captivity. By the time symptoms become obvious, your beardie may have been sick for days or even weeks.

Knowing the early warning signs can make the difference between a quick, straightforward treatment and a serious emergency. At South Pasadena Animal Hospital, we see bearded dragons regularly and know what to look for. Here are the 8 warning signs every owner should know.

1. Not Eating (Beyond Normal Causes)

Bearded dragons can skip meals for perfectly normal reasons. During brumation (a hibernation-like state), before a shed, or after moving to a new environment, a temporary drop in appetite is common and usually resolves on its own within a few days to a couple of weeks.

It becomes concerning when your beardie:

  • Refuses food for two or more weeks outside of brumation season
  • Is losing visible weight (especially along the tail base and hip bones)
  • Shows other symptoms alongside the appetite loss

Possible causes include intestinal parasites, mouth rot (infectious stomatitis), impaction from substrate or oversized food items, and metabolic bone disease. When in doubt, a vet visit can quickly rule out serious issues.

2. Persistent Lethargy

Healthy bearded dragons are active during the day. They bask, explore, watch their surroundings, and respond when you approach. If your beardie is sleeping all day, not basking under the heat lamp, or seems unresponsive, something may be off.

Lethargy can indicate infection, parasites, improper enclosure temperatures, or organ issues. Before rushing to the vet, check your husbandry first — make sure basking temps are 100–110°F, cool side is 80–85°F, and your UVB bulb isn't expired (they lose output before they burn out). If everything checks out and the lethargy persists more than a few days, it's time for a checkup.

3. Black Beard That Won't Go Away

Occasional black bearding is completely normal. Bearded dragons darken their throat when they feel territorial, stressed, or are trying to regulate body temperature.

What's not normal is a beard that stays persistently dark — especially combined with puffing up, lethargy, or loss of appetite. This sustained darkening often signals pain or internal discomfort. It's commonly seen alongside respiratory infections, gastrointestinal issues, or other sources of pain that aren't immediately visible.

4. Mouth Gaping, Mucus, or Wheezing

Bearded dragons sometimes gape their mouths while basking — that's normal thermoregulation. But open-mouth breathing when your beardie is not basking is a red flag.

  • Mucus or bubbles around the mouth or nostrils
  • Wheezing, clicking, or popping sounds when breathing
  • Excess saliva or stringy discharge
  • Puffing up the body while breathing (struggling for air)

These are signs of a respiratory infection, which can escalate quickly in reptiles. Respiratory infections are very treatable when caught early, but can become dangerous if left alone. Don't wait on this one.

5. Swollen or Closed Eyes

Puffy, swollen, or crusty eyes can indicate several problems:

  • One eye affected: possible abscess, injury, or retained shed around the eye
  • Both eyes affected: may point to a systemic issue, vitamin A deficiency, or lighting problems
  • Coil UVB bulbs (as opposed to tube-style) can cause eye irritation — if you're using a coil bulb, consider switching to a linear tube UVB

Eye issues should be evaluated by a vet rather than treated at home, as the underlying cause determines the right treatment.

6. Abnormal Stool or No Stool

Healthy bearded dragon poop has two parts: a brown solid portion and a white urate (the reptile equivalent of urine). Knowing what normal looks like helps you spot problems early.

See a vet if you notice:

  • Red or black stool (possible internal bleeding)
  • Extremely runny or foul-smelling stool (possible parasites or infection)
  • No stool for 1–2 weeks despite eating (possible impaction)

Impaction — a blockage in the digestive tract — is common in beardies. It can be caused by loose substrate (sand, crushed walnut), food items that are too large, or chronic dehydration. Mild cases sometimes resolve with warm baths and gentle belly massage, but persistent impaction needs veterinary attention.

7. Wobbly Walking or Limb Weakness

Shaky, wobbly movement, difficulty lifting the body off the ground, or rubbery-feeling limbs are classic signs of metabolic bone disease (MBD). This is one of the most common health issues we see in bearded dragons.

MBD is caused by insufficient calcium, vitamin D3, or UVB lighting. Without enough of these, the body pulls calcium from the bones, leading to soft, deformed bones over time. The jaw may feel soft or spongy. Limbs may appear bowed.

MBD is treatable if caught early — but can cause permanent skeletal damage if it progresses too far. Proper UVB lighting (10.0 or 12% tube bulb, replaced every 6 months) and calcium supplementation with D3 are essential for prevention.

8. Skin Discoloration or Stuck Shed

Bearded dragons shed their skin regularly, especially when growing. Most of the time it comes off cleanly. But retained shed — particularly around toes, tail tips, or eyes — can constrict blood flow and cause tissue damage if not addressed.

  • Dark patches on the belly: possible thermal burn from an unregulated heat source (heat rocks are dangerous — never use them)
  • Yellow or orange discoloration: may indicate yellow fungus disease, a serious fungal infection
  • Unusual lumps or bumps: could be abscesses, parasites, or growths that need evaluation

When Is It an Emergency?

Some situations can't wait for a scheduled appointment:

  • Prolapse — tissue protruding from the vent. Keep it moist with a damp cloth and get to a vet immediately.
  • Egg binding (in females) — straining, lethargy, swollen belly. Can be fatal without intervention.
  • Severe trauma — being dropped, stepped on, or attacked by another pet.
  • Seizures or complete paralysis — may indicate severe MBD, toxin exposure, or neurological issues.

What Happens at a Bearded Dragon Vet Visit?

If you've never brought a reptile to the vet, here's what to expect:

  • Physical exam: weight, body condition, mouth check, palpation, skin and eye assessment
  • Fecal test: checking for parasites — extremely common in beardies, especially from pet stores
  • Bloodwork: if needed, to check organ function and calcium levels
  • Husbandry review: we'll ask about your setup — temperatures, UVB type, diet, supplements — because many health issues trace back to husbandry

At SPAH, we see reptiles regularly and our vets are experienced in bearded dragon care. Check our pricing page for transparent exam costs.

Keeping Your Bearded Dragon Healthy

  • Annual wellness exams (twice yearly for seniors) catch issues before they become serious
  • Proper UVB lighting — use a linear tube bulb (Arcadia or ReptiSun 10.0) and replace every 6 months, even if it still turns on
  • Balanced diet appropriate for age: more insects for juveniles, more greens for adults
  • Calcium dusting with D3 on every other feeding
  • SoCal bonus: our natural sunlight is hugely beneficial for beardies. Supervised outdoor time in warm weather (above 75°F) provides natural UVB that no bulb can fully replicate

If you notice any of these signs, don't wait. Early treatment is almost always easier, less expensive, and better for your pet. We see bearded dragons and other reptiles at our Alhambra clinic and would be happy to help.
