Senior Cat Health: What Changes After 10

Pasadena has a large population of indoor cats living long, comfortable lives — in the bungalows of Bungalow Heaven National Historic District, in the PCC student apartments along Fair Oaks Ave, in the faculty housing near Caltech on Wilson and California, and in the older craftsman homes along Mentor Ave and Marengo Ave that have seen generations of families and their cats. Many of these cats are in their senior years, and senior cats have a very different set of health needs than younger ones.

The three conditions we screen for most carefully in senior Pasadena cats are hyperthyroidism, chronic kidney disease, and hypertension (high blood pressure). All three are common, all three are manageable when caught early, and all three can quietly progress for months before an owner notices anything is wrong.

For cats 10 and older, we recommend twice-yearly wellness visits that include a blood pressure check, thyroid level assessment, and a kidney function panel. The goal is to catch changes early — not to wait until your cat is noticeably sick.

Hyperthyroidism

The most common endocrine disorder in senior cats. Signs include weight loss despite eating well, increased vocalization, restlessness, and rapid heartbeat. Highly manageable with daily medication or dietary therapy once diagnosed.

Chronic Kidney Disease

Affects a significant portion of cats over 15. Early detection through bloodwork allows for dietary and hydration management that meaningfully slows progression. By the time symptoms are visible, disease is often advanced.

Dental Disease

Most cats develop dental disease by age three. It causes chronic pain that cats hide remarkably well. Professional cleanings under anesthesia address active disease; dental X-rays reveal problems below the gumline that surface exams miss.

Hypertension

High blood pressure in cats often develops secondary to kidney disease or hyperthyroidism. Left untreated, it can cause sudden blindness, neurological changes, and heart damage. We check blood pressure routinely in senior patients.

For the Cat Who Hates the Carrier

A Low-Stress Approach to Cat Exams

A lot of cat owners delay or skip vet visits because they know how stressful the experience is for their cat. We hear this regularly — and it's something we work hard to address.

We don't rush cats. We let them come out of the carrier on their own terms, keep handling to what's medically necessary, and keep the exam room quiet. If a cat is too stressed to examine safely, we discuss options — including pre-visit calming strategies — rather than forcing a stressful interaction that won't produce useful information anyway.

We also give practical pre-visit tips: leaving the carrier out in the home a few days before the appointment, placing familiar bedding inside, and covering the carrier during transport. Small adjustments that make a real difference for anxious cats.

What to Expect at Your Cat's First Visit

1

Check-in & settle

We greet you and your cat by name. Your cat is allowed to stay in their carrier until they're ready — no pulling them out in the waiting area.

2

History & intake

Your veterinarian reviews any records you've brought, asks about diet, behavior, litter box habits, and anything you've noticed — then builds a picture of your cat's baseline health.

3

Physical exam

A thorough head-to-tail exam: weight, heart rate, respiratory rate, lymph nodes, eyes, ears, mouth, dental grade, coat and skin, abdomen palpation. Unhurried. Your cat sets the pace.

4

Discussion & care plan

We walk you through everything we found, what we recommend, and what it costs — before we proceed with anything. Questions are welcomed, not rushed.

5

Checkout & follow-up

You receive a visit summary and any prescriptions needed. Our online pharmacy handles refills and home delivery between visits.

Frequently Asked Questions

From Pasadena cat owners we see regularly.

How often should my Pasadena cat see a vet?

Once yearly for adult cats in good health, and twice yearly for senior cats 10 years and older. Cats that spend any time outdoors benefit from more frequent parasite screening given Pasadena's local wildlife and rodent population.

What are the signs my cat might have hyperthyroidism?

Common signs include unexplained weight loss despite eating well, increased vocalization, restlessness, a rapid or irregular heartbeat, and increased thirst and urination. Hyperthyroidism is one of the most common conditions in senior cats and is highly manageable when caught early through routine bloodwork.

My cat hates the vet. What does SPAH do differently?

We take a low-stress approach — we let cats come out of their carrier on their own terms, keep handling to what's medically necessary, and maintain a calm exam environment. We also provide pre-visit guidance: leaving the carrier out at home several days before, placing familiar bedding inside, and covering the carrier during transport all make a meaningful difference.

Does dental disease really affect indoor cats?

Yes. Indoor cats are just as susceptible as outdoor cats — by age three most cats have some degree of dental disease Cats hide dental pain well, often continuing to eat despite significant discomfort. Professional cleanings under anesthesia address active disease; dental X-rays reveal problems below the gumline that a visual exam can't catch.
