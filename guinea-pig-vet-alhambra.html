What Actually Sends Guinea Pigs to Our Exam Table

Ask us what catches guinea pig owners off guard most often and the answer is dental disease, every time. Those teeth never stop growing — that part is normal. The trouble starts when the wear goes uneven, usually because the diet is short on the coarse hay that grinds them down.

The insidious part is that you can't see it. The molars sit at the back of the mouth, behind the incisors you can see, and a pig with painful spurs on those molars looks fine from the front. What owners notice first is weight loss and drooling — a wet chin, a damp chest. By the time a pig is visibly struggling to eat, it's usually been building for months. If your guinea pig is dropping food, eating slower, or suddenly picky about hay, look past the front teeth and call us.

Then there's the sneezing, which is the other call we get constantly. A sneezing guinea pig is not a guinea pig with a cold. Respiratory infections in this species can move fast — a pig that's a little snuffly on Monday can be in real trouble by Wednesday. Add labored breathing or a clicking sound and that's a same-day call, not a wait-and-see. We wrote more about that in our post on guinea pig sneezing.

The third one is entirely preventable and entirely on us as owners: vitamin C. Guinea pigs can't make their own, so it has to come in daily, every day, for life. Pellets lose their C potency as they sit in the bag, which is why we ask what brand you buy and how long ago you opened it — that question surprises people, and it's often where the answer is.

The ones that come up less often but matter

Calcium-heavy diets are a common thread behind bladder sludge and stones — too much kale, too much spinach, calcium-enriched pellets. Watch for straining, blood in the urine, wet fur around the rear, or a hunched posture. Some stones need surgery, so this isn't one to sit on.

Mites are worse in guinea pigs than most owners expect. We see frantic scratching, hair loss, and sores from self-trauma, and the severe cases can look almost neurological — pigs having seizure-like episodes from the sheer discomfort. It's treatable, and it's miserable until it's treated. Please don't reach for an over-the-counter product; several are genuinely dangerous for this species, and so are a number of common antibiotics.

Why we put every pig on the scale

Every guinea pig, every visit, no exceptions. Weight loss hides under that coat — a pig can drop a meaningful percentage of its body weight and still look like the same animal across the room. Hands are better than eyes here, and a scale is better than hands. Tracked over time, that number tells us something is wrong weeks before anything else does. A home kitchen scale and a note on your phone once a week is genuinely one of the most useful things you can do for your pig.

Beyond the scale, an annual for a guinea pig means a real hands-on exam: dental assessment, heart and lungs, abdominal palpation, coat and skin, and a proper conversation about diet and housing. Prey animals conceal illness — that's the whole design — so the annual is often the only chance to catch something while it's still small.

When to call us the same day

Not eating for more than 12–24 hours. Labored breathing or clicking sounds. Straining to urinate, or blood in the urine. Frantic seizure-like scratching. Sudden severe lethargy. A new lump or a swollen belly. Any of those, call us at (626) 441-1314 rather than watching it overnight — guinea pigs don't leave much margin.

Guinea Pig Vet FAQ — Alhambra

How often should my guinea pig see a vet?

At least once a year for a wellness exam. Guinea pigs over 4 years old should be seen every 6 months since they're more prone to dental disease, bladder stones, and tumors as they age.

My guinea pig is sneezing — should I be worried?

Occasional sneezing from dust or bedding is normal, but persistent sneezing, nasal discharge, wheezing, or crusty eyes can signal an upper respiratory infection that needs veterinary treatment. Read our guide to guinea pig sneezing for more details.

Why are some antibiotics dangerous for guinea pigs?

Guinea pigs have a delicate gut microbiome, and certain antibiotics (like amoxicillin and penicillin) can destroy beneficial gut bacteria, causing fatal diarrhea. Our vets only use guinea pig-safe antibiotics and are well aware of which medications to avoid.

Does my guinea pig need vitamin C supplements?

Yes. Unlike most mammals, guinea pigs cannot synthesize vitamin C and must get it from their diet. We recommend 25-50mg of vitamin C daily through fresh veggies (bell peppers, parsley) and a liquid supplement if needed.

What bedding is best for guinea pigs?

We recommend paper-based bedding (like Carefresh) or fleece liners. Avoid cedar and pine shavings, which release aromatic oils that can irritate guinea pig respiratory systems and contribute to URIs.
