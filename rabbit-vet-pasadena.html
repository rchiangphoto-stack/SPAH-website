Local Knowledge

Rabbit Health Conditions We Treat

These are the most common reasons Pasadena rabbit owners visit SPAH — and the conditions we're most attentive to at every wellness visit.

GI Stasis — A Same-Day Emergency

When a rabbit's digestive system slows or stops, it can become life-threatening within hours. Early signs include fewer or smaller droppings, loss of appetite, sitting hunched, or tooth grinding. If you see any of these, contact us the same day — do not wait overnight to see if it improves on its own.

Dental Malocclusion & Spurs

Rabbit teeth grow continuously throughout their life. Without proper hay intake and normal wear patterns, molars can develop sharp spurs that cut into the tongue and cheeks — causing pain that prevents eating. Many rabbits hide dental pain extremely well. We check molar alignment and wear at every exam.

Uterine Cancer in Unspayed Females

Unspayed female rabbits have a very high lifetime risk of uterine cancer. It's one of the most common and most preventable serious health conditions in domestic rabbits. We strongly recommend spaying female rabbits around 4–6 months of age, before the risk begins to climb significantly.

Heat Stroke

Rabbits don't pant or sweat effectively. In Pasadena's summer heat, even indoor temperatures in an un-air-conditioned room can be dangerous. Signs include rapid breathing, wet nose, lethargy, or lying on their side. This is an emergency. Contact a vet immediately.

What We Offer

Rabbit Veterinary Services at SPAH

Wellness Exams

Annual (or twice-yearly for rabbits 5+) physical exam: weight, teeth, gut sounds, eyes, ears, coat, and husbandry review.

Dental Checks

Assessment of incisors and back molars for spurs, malocclusion, and root problems. X-rays when indicated.

Spay & Neuter

Spaying removes the risk of uterine cancer in females. Neutering reduces territorial behavior in males. Recommended at 4–6 months.

GI Assessment

Gut sounds auscultation, radiographs when needed, and treatment for GI motility problems including stasis.

Bloodwork & Urinalysis

In-house diagnostics to assess kidney function, liver health, and general organ status — especially important in senior rabbits.

Husbandry Counseling

Diet, hay intake, housing, temperature management, and enrichment — particularly relevant for Pasadena's summer climate.

Frequently Asked Questions

From Pasadena rabbit owners we see regularly.

Does SPAH have experience treating rabbits?

Yes. Rabbits and small exotic mammals are a regular part of our practice. Dr. Gina Navia has particular interest in exotic and small mammal care. We see rabbits for wellness exams, dental disease, GI stasis, spay/neuter, and other conditions specific to rabbit health — not as occasional patients, but consistently.

How do Pasadena summers affect house rabbits?

Rabbits are extremely sensitive to heat and can develop heat stroke at temperatures above 80–85°F — far lower than most people expect. Pasadena regularly exceeds 100°F in summer. Homes without reliable air conditioning can become dangerous for rabbits during heat waves. We discuss cooling strategies and heat emergency signs at every warm-season wellness visit.

What is GI stasis and how do I know if my rabbit has it?

GI stasis is when a rabbit's digestive tract slows down or stops. Signs include reduced or absent fecal droppings, not eating, a hunched posture, grinding teeth, or a distended abdomen. GI stasis can become life-threatening within hours. If you notice these signs, contact a vet the same day — do not wait to see if it improves on its own.

Should I spay my female rabbit?

We strongly recommend it. Unspayed female rabbits have a very high lifetime risk of uterine cancer — it's one of the most common preventable health problems we see in intact female rabbits. Spaying removes this risk — we literally remove the organ it originates from. The procedure is typically recommended around 4–6 months of age with proper anesthetic protocol.
